## What this page does

The headline is the piece that decides whether the carousel goes viral or disappears in the feed. R2 generates **10 candidate headlines** internally, runs each one through the **editorial verdict** on this page, and delivers only the best one to the carousel.

The patterns here were extracted from an analysis of **56 hooks with more than 10,000 likes** on Brazilian Instagram. It is not theory — it is what verifiably moved the needle.

## Patterns with negative lift (avoid)

| Pattern | Why it doesn't work |
|--------|---------------------|
| Direct statement | States without provoking curiosity |
| Generic reveal | "Discover", "Learn", "Meet" — saturated format |
| List / Tips / Number | Dead format, no tension |
| Empty motivational | No data, no conflict, no personality |

## The 6 emotional triggers

Each headline needs to activate **at least 2 triggers simultaneously**.

| Trigger | What it activates |
|---------|------------|
| **Nostalgia** | Affective memory, "how it used to be" |
| **Fear / Alert** | Urgency, something at risk |
| **Indignation** | Outrage, "this is wrong" |
| **Identity** | "This is about me, my generation" |
| **Curiosity** | Information gap that must be closed |
| **Aspiration** | Desire to be, to have, to reach |

### Proven high-performance combinations
- Nostalgia + Identity
- Fear + Generational
- Brazil + Identity
- Curiosity + Nostalgia

---

## Trigger words with best performance

`brazil` · `new` · `investigating` · `crisis` · `generation` · `instagram` · `why` · `how` · `nobody` · `the data` · `the rule` · `the turn` · `the way` · `the mistake`

`death` and `end` are high-impact words, but **they are not the default standard**. Use at most 1 time among the 10 candidates, and only when the topic really involves loss, closure, collapse, substitution or end of a cycle. Do not use as automatic dramatization.

## Headline families (choose 1 per carousel)

### Family 1 — Brazil / National Context

Structure: `[Phenomenon] in Brazil: [what this reveals]`

Use when the topic gains strength because it touches Brazilian behavior, class, territory, popular culture, consumption, religion, work or everyday politics.

Examples:
- "The Brazilian phenomenon that broke the algorithm: why a friar at 4am entered the national routine"
- "Brazil's new elite runs at 6am: what the running clubs reveal about status"
- "The Brazilian way of using AI: improvisation, workaround and a new dispute over repertoire"

### Family 2 — Generational Question

Structure: `Why is [Generation] [Unexpected Behavior]?`

Examples:
- "Why are Millennials having mid-life crises in their 30s?"
- "Why has Gen Z stopped wearing the badge and started treating employment as a contract?"
- "The generation that grew up online is going offline: why this matters for brands?"

### Family 3 — Investigating [Phenomenon]

Structure: `Investigating [cultural or behavioral phenomenon]`

Examples:
- "Investigating daytime parties in coffee shops"
- "Investigating parents who are raising children with landlines"
- "Investigating luxury gyms as a new status symbol"

### Family 4 — [Name/Brand] + [Unexpected Reveal]

Structure: recognizable name + surprising action or fact

Examples:
- "Jaden Smith opened a restaurant where nobody pays: the model that confronts capitalism"
- "How Adidas became a symbol of Soviet youth in post-communism?"
- "How did Tiny Desk become the coolest music experience?"

### Family 5 — Two-Dots Formula

Structure: `[Provocative framing]: [Curiosity hook]`

Sentence 1 redefines the phenomenon. Sentence 2 creates an informational gap.

Examples:
- "Running took the place of the night out: why Gen Z swapped the bar for asphalt at 6am"
- "The data no guru wants to admit: posts about niche perform 4x worse than posts about culture"
- "The new dispute over repertoire: why AI stopped being a tool and started filtering taste"

### Family 6 — Counterintuitive Data

Structure: `The data that [contradicts common sense]: [human or market implication]`

Examples:
- "The data that overturns the content manual: talking less about your niche can generate more authority"
- "The metric no one shows: reach without emotional identification turns into traffic without memory"
- "The number behind the trend: why behavior beat the category"

### Family 7 — Contrast / Before and After

Structure: `[Concrete before]. Now [new behavior]. [Open tension].`

Examples:
- "Before, status was a full restaurant. Now it's a running club at 6am. Consumption changed scenery."
- "Before, creativity was hidden repertoire. Now it's a public prompt. The differentiator changed places."
- "Before, brands disputed attention. Now they dispute belonging."

### Family 8 — Rupture / End / Crisis

Structure: `[Real rupture]: [what replaces the old model]`

Use with care. This family only enters when there's evidence of substitution, collapse, end of cycle or concrete crisis. Prefer `fall`, `turn`, `collapse`, `swap`, `exhaustion`, `end of cycle` before `death`.

Examples:
- "The fall of generic content: why posts without a point of view stopped traveling"
- "The exhaustion of lifestyle influencers: what shows up in their place"
- "The crisis of personal taste: how the algorithm leveled choices that seemed intimate"

## Mandatory distribution of the 10 candidate headlines

When R2 generates the 10 candidates, it distributes them like this:

| # | Type | Format |
|---|------|---------|
| 1 | National context | Family 1 |
| 2 | Generational question | Family 2 |
| 3 | Cultural investigation | Family 3 |
| 4 | Brand/character in contradiction | Family 4 |
| 5 | Two-dots reframing | Family 5 |
| 6 | Counterintuitive data | Family 6 |
| 7 | Before/after contrast | Family 7 |
| 8 | Real rupture | Family 8, if it fits; otherwise Family 5 |
| 9 | Magnetic Narrative | Format below |
| 10 | Magnetic Narrative | Format below |

### Anti-repetition rule

- The 10 candidates need to cover at least 6 different families.
- `death` or `end` appear in at most 1 candidate.
- If the previous carousel already used `death`, `end` or Family 8 as winner, this run can only use Family 8 if the topic literally demands it.
- The winner cannot repeat the same family as the previous run when there's an approved alternative with up to 10% lower score.
- If two headlines tie, choose the one more specific to the audience and less similar to previous carousels.

### Editorial form of families 1–8

Each candidate must follow the structure of its family. Don't force two-dots when the family calls for a question, data, contrast or investigation.

When using two-dots, the rule is: sentence 1 redefines the phenomenon; sentence 2 opens a real gap of curiosity.

Correct examples:
- "Running took the place of the night out: why Gen Z swapped the bar for asphalt at 6am"
- "The data no guru wants to admit: posts about the niche perform 4x worse than posts about culture"
- "Paid influencer defending a company is not advertising: it's a digital militia testing whether the Central Bank falls with election tactics"

Wrong examples (reject):
- ❌ "Gyms reopened. No one stopped running." — no two-dots, no hook
- ❌ "Running is Brazil's new phenomenon" — direct statement
- ❌ "Why everyone is running" — generic question without reframing

### Magnetic Narrative format (options 9–10)

Strict structure: **3 sentences with periods** — `[Concrete scene]. [Mechanism]. [Open tension].`

Sentence 1 describes what happened. Sentence 2 explains how it works. Sentence 3 opens tension.

Correct examples:
- "Padre Reginaldo runs a prayer livestream every day at 4am. He has more simultaneous viewers than a professional streamer. It didn't go viral, it created routine — people wake up for it."
- "Hoka tripled its revenue in Brazil three years in a row. No lifestyle influencer recommended it. Word of mouth came out of the running clubs."

## Headline verdict — internal checklist

Before including a headline in the list of 10 candidates, R2 runs this verdict **internally** (never shown to the user):

### Dimension 1 — Lift patterns present
Minimum 1 required:
- [ ] Brazil / National Context?
- [ ] Real Rupture / Change / Crisis?
- [ ] Generational?
- [ ] Novelty?
- [ ] Counterintuitive data?
- [ ] Character/Brand in contradiction?

If zero patterns → rewrite the headline.

### Dimension 2 — Emotional triggers
Minimum 2 triggers simultaneously. If only 1 → emotionally weak. If zero → fail.

### Dimension 3 — Rejection checklist

Fail if it falls into any:

- ❌ Direct statement (states without provoking)
- ❌ Generic reveal ("discover", "learn", "meet")
- ❌ List / number of items ("5 tips for...")
- ❌ Empty motivational
- ❌ AI tone (test by reading aloud)
- ❌ "The death of..." as automatic dramatization
- ❌ "The end of..." without real evidence of substitution or closure
- ❌ Forbidden constructions: "when X becomes Y", "the rise of", "the impact of", "why X is changing", "not X, it's Y", "became" as main verb

### Final verdict
```
Active patterns: [list]
Active triggers: [list]
Anti-patterns: [list or "none"]
Status: APPROVED / REJECTED
```

**Rejected headlines are rewritten, not removed.** The total delivered to the final selection is always exactly 10 approved headlines.

## How R2 picks the winner from the 10

Applies automatic ranking:

1. **Number of lift patterns** (more patterns = better)
2. **Number of emotional triggers** (more triggers = better, minimum 2)
3. **Proven trigger combination** (Nostalgia+Identity, Fear+Generational, Brazil+Identity, Curiosity+Nostalgia get extra weight)
4. **Specificity to the audience** (the more concrete, the better)
5. **Variety relative to previous runs** (do not repeat the winning family)
6. **Presence of trigger word without cliché** (brazil/new/investigating/crisis/generation/instagram/why/how/the data/the rule)

In case of a technical tie, picks the one that best matches the **narrative axis** of the news (Culture, Market, News, Cases, Product).

`death` and `end` do not get extra points in the ranking. They only pass when they are semantically true and don't repeat recent carousels.

## Cover headline — special rules

The chosen headline goes to the cover, but it has to **fit visually in up to 5 lines** in the cover typography (88px). If it doesn't fit:

**Preference 1:** Use the full headline intact.

**Preference 2:** Derive a short version BUT keeping the original family. If it had two-dots, the short version also has it. If it was a question, it stays a question. If it was the "Investigating" pattern, it stays "Investigating".

### Examples of correct derivation

**Original:** "Investigating the tribe that swapped the bar for asphalt: what running clubs reveal about the generation that can't stop"

**Short cover:** "INVESTIGATING THE TRIBE THAT SWAPPED THE BAR FOR ASPHALT"

(Shortened but kept the "Investigating X" pattern.)

### Wrong derivation

- ❌ "THE NEW NIGHTLIFE STARTS AT 6AM" — killed the two-dots and the hook
- ❌ "THE CLUB THAT TOOK THE STREETS" — turned into a generic statement
