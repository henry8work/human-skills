# 18 — Design Director of Human DNA

This module defines the mandatory visual standard of the `resultado/DNA.pdf`.

The DNA can't look like a report, exported markdown, a bureaucratic document or a PDF assembled by automation without taste. It must work as an analytical visual presentation, with art direction, grid, typography, contrast, rhythm and intent.

Before rendering, also read `19-Layout-Composition-Training.md`. The Design Director defines the mandatory standard; the advanced training brings practical repertoire absorbed from carousel, presentations, photography, motion, storyboard, template design and editorial.

## 0. Rules that come before any pixel

Three failures reject an entire DNA, no matter how beautiful the page looks. They come before grid, color, typography or composition. If any one happens, the document is rejected even if everything else is perfect.

### 0.1 The document is brand-driven, never template-driven

Background, text ink, title, accent, neutrals, typography and rhythm are born **from the analyzed brand** — never from a fixed renderer theme. A pink-red gradient background (or any color) that didn't come from the brand's real palette is a critical failure, even if the page looks nice. If the brand is light and earthy, the document is light and earthy; if it's dark and harsh, the document follows. The PDF's visual system is the **first proof** that the brand was understood.

Before laying out, the Maestro fixes the document's chromatic system deriving it from the real colors: background, ink, title, accent and neutrals (paper palette). Two DNAs of different brands can't come out with the same face. A renderer that applies the same generic visual to distinct brands is rejected: it decorates, it doesn't interpret.

### 0.2 Zero example content — only the real brand

No line of matrix, swatch, reference, persona or sentence can contain **generic template example**. Texts like `Aesop, COS, Kinfolk`, `Sofia Coppola, Wong Kar-wai`, `off-white, washed black, warm gray` or `Direct, sensitive, visual, intelligent` exist in templates and protocols **only as reasoning examples for the Maestro** — never as PDF content.

In the final document, each cell is filled with what was **observed** in the brand, **declared** by the person or **proposed** by the Maestro (and a proposal comes marked as `[proposal]`). If there's no real data for a line, the Maestro doesn't invent a catalog example: it either extracts from the brand, marks as a proposal to validate, or removes the line. A matrix filled with template example **rejects the entire page** — it lies about having analyzed the brand. Before closing the PDF, scan each page: if any reference, color or sentence would serve identically for any brand of the same template, it's generic and needs to become a real read of the brand.

### 0.3 Page economy — fewer, stronger pages

The DNA.pdf **doesn't grow to impress**. Target: **14 to 22 pages** for most brands. Only exceeds that when the real visual archive justifies it (many valid photos becoming more editorial boards) — never to paginate more text. Depth lives in the `DNA.md`; the PDF consolidates.

A PDF of 40, 50 or 60+ pages is almost always paginated markdown dump, not art direction. Before opening a new page, the question is always: "does this consolidate into a better composition on the previous page?". **Consolidating beats expanding.** Related short topics become a matrix, cards or numbered list on the same page; not three almost-empty pages.

### 0.4 The three depth matrices are the spine — with the brand inside

When the material allows, every DNA delivers three editorial matrices, and each cell brings **the real brand**, not an example:

- **References** — brands, directors/photographers, design/editorial, cinema, culture: the concrete worlds that explain **this** brand.
- **Tone of voice** — general tone, rhythm, vocabulary, avoid, humor, positioning: extracted from the real text samples.
- **Visual** — palette, aesthetics, composition, light, texture, references: read from the real visual material.

Each line carries **extracted reading + meaning + practical rule + usage limit**, never a copied template example. A matrix without the brand inside is decoration, and decoration rejects.

## Role

The Maestro acts as a designer, art director, visual editor and creative analyst before rendering the PDF.

It must think like:

- editorial designer;
- presentation designer;
- art director;
- typography specialist;
- photography analyst;
- visual systems designer;
- UI/UX reviewer;
- magazine editor.

## Thinking correction

Every visual user critique must turn into a thinking rule, not just a local correction.

When a page is flagged as ugly, poor, badly spaced, glued, empty, repetitive, markdown-looking, with photo used as decoration, with technical code, with raw table, with number far from the label or with any design failure, the Maestro must:

- fix the rendering or the affected document;
- identify which visual principle failed;
- update this module or the corresponding protocol;
- re-validate the PDF against the new rule;
- consider the error as forbidden in all future DNAs.

The Design Director isn't a post-processing. It governs the way of thinking before writing, laying out, selecting image, grouping text, choosing icon, positioning number or exporting PDF.

## Decision order before the pixel

The Maestro never jumps from intent straight to layout. Every page passes through five decisions, in this order:

1. **Message:** what does this page need to make the person understand, feel or decide?
2. **Hierarchy:** what does the person read first, second and last?
3. **Structure:** what type of page best serves the content: matrix, gallery, big data, application rule, two columns, hero, comparison or synthesis?
4. **Composition:** where does each block land on the grid, where does it breathe, where does it create tension and how does it lead the eye?
5. **Finishing:** final typography, weight, color, swatch, icon, thin line, caption and micro-details.

If the page doesn't yet have a dominant message, it can't be laid out. If the Maestro is choosing color, icon or effect before defining the hierarchy, it's starting in the wrong place.

## Absolute rules

- Aesthetics aren't finishing. They're a central part of perceived quality.
- Depth isn't volume. The document must be direct, dense and well-consolidated.
- Each page must have an assertion and a visual or textual proof.
- Every page must have three reading levels: anchor, context and metadata.
- The PDF must win the thumbnail test: coherent system, distinct rhythms and no page looking like filler.
- The visual system comes before the page: palette, typography, grid, margin, components and image treatment must be defined.
- Text can never look like markdown.
- A list can never become a running paragraph.
- A topic always becomes a layout component.
- An image can't be thrown on the page.
- An image can't be cut without intent.
- A page can't have an accidental gap.
- Data and label must form a single visual block. Never leave the number far from the text that explains the number.
- Empty space must be composition, not abandonment.
- Typography must have real hierarchy.
- Spelling, accents and punctuation must be correct. If the font doesn't support accents, the font is wrong.
- Contrast must exist in size, weight, color and position.
- Each page must have a clear role in the narrative.
- No page can exist only to fill space.
- No deep analysis can become a giant manual without direction.

## Page criteria

Every PDF page must pass these criteria:

1. **Margin:** is there consistent, deliberate margin?
2. **Grid:** do the blocks obey a visible structure?
3. **Rule of thirds:** do image and text occupy zones with visual tension?
4. **Breathing:** is there space between blocks, not just random empty space?
5. **Contrast:** do title, body, caption and metadata have distinct weights?
6. **Typography:** does the font talk to the brand or to a justified approximation?
7. **Language:** are accents, punctuation and spelling correct?
8. **Grouping:** are number, unit and label close enough to be read as a single piece of information?
9. **Context:** does the page explain what it's showing?
10. **Image:** were product, face, lettering, packaging and important information preserved?
11. **Rhythm:** is this page different enough from the previous one to keep reading alive?
12. **Perception:** does the page look expensive, careful and trustworthy?
13. **Assertion:** does the page defend a clear conclusion, not just a generic theme?
14. **Proof:** is there visible evidence supporting the assertion?
15. **Reading:** do anchor, context and metadata appear in different weights?

If the answer is "no" to any critical item, the page must be redesigned.

## Perceptual principles

The layout must respect how the human eye organizes information. These rules are mandatory:

- **Proximity:** related elements stay close. Number, unit and explanation form a single block.
- **Similarity:** elements with the same function use similar shape, size and style. If a card is an exception, the difference must be intentional.
- **Continuity:** alignments lead the reading. Edges of text, image and caption must create invisible lines.
- **Figure and ground:** the main object must separate from the background. Breathing isn't leftover; it's what allows the eye to recognize the figure.
- **Entry point:** each page has a single dominant element. Two elements fighting for attention leave the page confusing.
- **Asymmetric balance:** internal pages can have tension and movement, but visual weight must be resolved. Deliberate tension is expressive; accidental tension looks like error.

The Maestro must design the eye's path. The person must understand where to start without being instructed.

## Minimum spatial system

The PDF uses presentation format, with editorial logic.

Reference numeric rules:

- Standard format: 16:9.
- Minimum margin: 64 px.
- Ideal margin: 72 to 96 px.
- Base grid: 12 columns.
- Gutter between columns: 24 px.
- Baseline: multiples of 8 px.
- Small internal spaces: 8, 16 or 24 px.
- Separation between different groups: 48 px or more.

These numbers aren't for appearing in the PDF. They guide the rendering. If a text is glued, a number is far from the label or a page looks loose, first review proximity, margin, grid and baseline.

Margin variations are only allowed by page type, not by improvised adjustment. Visual hero can bleed to the edge, but texts, captions and reading elements still respect safe margin.

## Text as layout

Markdown is only content source. The PDF must not show the markdown structure.

Mandatory conversion:

- `#` becomes chapter cover or editorial title;
- `##` becomes section opening;
- bullets become cards, numbered lines, columns or modules;
- tables become laid-out matrices;
- long paragraphs become extra pages, short blocks or syntheses;
- technical excerpts become visual explanations;
- lists with many items become sequence, not single block.
- repeated content becomes synthesis;
- raw detail stays in `DNA.md`, while the PDF shows the intelligent consolidation.

Forbidden:

- bullets glued in one line;
- loose markdown symbols;
- paragraph with topics separated by small squares;
- raw table;
- text glued to image;
- page with text too small for presentation reading.
- visible sentence starting with lowercase;
- fragment that only makes sense to someone who knows design, like "two accents in the same piece", without explaining the expected action.

## English and didactics

The PDF must be written for a real person, not for another agent.

Non-negotiable rule:

- Every visible sentence starts with a capital letter.
- Every sentence has correct punctuation.
- Every topic must make sense on its own.
- Every instruction must say what to do and why it matters.
- Design, color, typography, contrast, grid and art direction terms must be explained with warmth.

Bad example: `Two accents in the same piece.`

Good example: `Avoid using two accent colors on the same piece, because they compete with each other and leave the visual direction confusing. Choose one highlight color at a time.`

If a sentence doesn't teach anything to a person without technical knowledge, it must be rewritten before entering the PDF.

## Translation of technical content

The DNA can't dump a technical term without teaching what to do with it.

Whenever WCAG, contrast 4.5:1, RGB, HEX, baseline, modular scale, safezone, aspect ratio, CSS, variable, fallback, DPI, render, crop, grid or any technical term appears, the Maestro needs to convert into practical guidance:

- **What it means:** simple explanation, in one sentence.
- **How to apply:** direct usage instruction.
- **Approved example:** a real brand situation.
- **Common error:** what not to do.

Bad example: `WCAG AA — normal text ≥ 4.5:1`.

Good example: `Contrast is the difference between text and background. Use dark text on light background and light text on dark background so reading is easy on mobile. At NOWDAYS, ink on cream is safe for editorial pages; sativa on terracotta should be avoided because it loses reading and looks amateur.`

A technical term only enters the PDF if it becomes a practical decision. If it doesn't help the reader apply the brand, it stays out of the PDF or goes into `DNA.md` with explanation.

## Intelligent consolidation

The PDF must be a reading presentation, not a dump of `DNA.md`.

The Maestro must:

- remove operational metadata and internal instructions that don't serve the reader;
- select what's essential for creative decision;
- consolidate repeated findings into one clear rule;
- transform long analysis into matrix, topic or synthesis slide;
- keep the PDF objective, visual and navigable;
- avoid empty pages, almost-empty pages and pages created only to "breathe";
- prefer 1 strong page over 4 weak pages;
- use the `DNA.md` as complete source and the `DNA.pdf` as presentation material.
- group short topics on the same page when there's space;
- prevent almost-empty slides when the content fits into a single composition.

A good presentation has rhythm, not excess. If the analysis is deep, the consolidation needs to be even smarter.

Do not include in the PDF: "master file", "every AI reads first", generation date, version, technical history, agent instructions or any metadata that doesn't help the user understand and apply the brand.

## Pagination and density

Pagination is an art-direction decision, not a markdown side-effect.

Before creating a new page, the Maestro needs to ask: does this content fit together in a better composition? If the answer is yes, it's mandatory to consolidate.

Rules:

- related short topics must become a single page with compact matrix, three columns, numbered list or small cards;
- two pages with little content each must be merged when they belong to the same subject;
- section continuity is only acceptable when the first page is visually full or when the reading really asks for a pause;
- if a page has less than 45% useful occupancy and isn't a cover, divider or hero image, it's rejected;
- don't break a page just because there's a new subtitle in the `DNA.md`;
- small subtitles can become lines, groups or labels within the same page;
- don't create empty chapter dividers for a common section; chapter opening only exists if it brings context, image, synthesis or real narrative shift;
- low-level sibling subtitles must become internal labels on the same page when content is short;
- the renderer must measure visual density before breaking: number of points, average length, number of lines and remaining useful space;
- tables must try compact readable mode before creating continuation; continuation with one or two lines is a pagination failure;
- dense pages must remain readable, but empty pages convey perception of laziness.

The goal is fewer stronger pages, not more pages filled any which way. Consolidating doesn't mean cramming. It means choosing the right structure for the real volume of information.

## Layout variation

It's forbidden to always use the same slide model.

The PDF must alternate ways of seeing information. Repetition creates a sense of template, laziness and low art direction. The Maestro must vary composition according to content, not decoration.

Expected page types, alternated with intent:

- editorial cover;
- chapter opening;
- editorial matrix/table;
- asymmetric cards;
- vertical numbered list;
- two dense columns;
- page with big data and short explanation;
- visual board/mosaic;
- image hero;
- enlarged detail;
- page with icons or swatches only when they explain the content;
- quote/strong sentence page;
- compact executive summary;
- application rule with do/don't.

Practical rule: if three pages in a row look like they have the same structure, the third needs to change. Changing doesn't mean swapping background color. It means changing hierarchy, position, density, scale, rhythm, use of image, icon, matrix or composition.

The renderer must have structural variations. The Maestro must choose the variation that best explains the content: short insight asks for big data; operational list asks for numbered lines; parallel concepts ask for matrix; visual evidence asks for board; practical rule asks for do/don't; argumentative text asks for two columns or synthesis.

Icons, swatches, lines and graphic details can't be loose decoration or appear as standard frame on every page. Each visual element must be tied to the page's content. Icon illustrates a specific idea. Swatch appears when the page talks about color or when a hexadecimal needs to be shown. Don't create a fixed side column with "DNA signals" or repeated elements without function.

It's forbidden to create a side rail, visual ruler, decorative column, "DNA SIGNALS" block, set of generic icons, decorative quotes, target, grid, palette or loose swatches to fill space. If the element doesn't directly explain that page's content, it doesn't enter. This pattern must be removed from the renderer, not just hidden by condition.

## Standard editorial script for the DNA.pdf

The PDF needs to have a reading narrative. The standard script has 17 base pages and can be compacted or expanded as the real material allows, but the mental order must be preserved: transparency, visual proof, interpretation, voice, references and synthesis.

1. **Cover:** brand name, visual promise and immediate tone.
2. **Resources analyzed:** transparency matrix showing what was effectively searched: site, Instagram, packaging, store, sent material, portfolio, blog, PDF, deck, photos and files. Each source must have status and main finding. If Instagram exists, it appears here with number of posts observed, visual grid, recurring palette and caption tone. If it doesn't exist or wasn't accessible, the PDF says that honestly.
3. **Photo gallery:** mosaic or board with real curated images. Shows the universe before explaining it. When material comes from Instagram, the observed grid becomes an editorial board.
4. **Enlarged detail:** strong crop of an image, explaining texture, light, gesture, product, surface or atmosphere.
5. **Central thesis:** sentence that summarizes the brand, extracted from general tone, positioning and visual evidence.
6. **Positioning:** 2×2 map or equivalent composition showing where the brand lives.
7. **Visual identity opening:** short visual transition, never an empty divider.
8. **Palette:** swatch, hex, paper, proportion and usage rule of each color.
9. **Color in use:** matrix showing where each color acts: background, title, text, CTA, accent, surface and limit.
10. **Aesthetic and composition:** two columns or diagram with visual feel, breathing, asymmetry, grid and way of occupying space.
11. **Light direction:** image + text or diagram explaining light, shadow, temperature, contrast and application.
12. **Texture:** enlarged detail of grain, skin, paper, metal, fabric, imperfection or finish.
13. **Voice opening:** short transition to verbal behavior.
14. **Voice principles:** triad of pillars with general tone, rhythm and humor.
15. **Vocabulary use × avoid:** do/don't page with terms, sentences and reasons.
16. **References:** reference worlds in matrix: brands, directors/photographers, design/editorial, cinema and culture. It must be clear when they're aspirations, not the brand's own material.
17. **Executive summary:** essence, color, typography, photography, voice, limit and final rule on one page.

This script is base, not straitjacket. If a brand has a lot of photography, there can be more pages of gallery, detail and photographic direction. If there's no Instagram, the resources-analyzed page records the absence. If there are no own photos, the PDF uses aspirational references and makes the origin clear. What can't happen is skipping source transparency, hiding gaps, starting with abstract text, or leaving the visual universe only for the end.

## Images

Images are editorial raw material.

Photo is real photography. Text, screenshot, UI, card, graphic, banner, layout and piece with text aren't a photo. These materials can enter as visual assets or applications, but they don't enter the photographic analysis. Logo enters its own category of visual signature.

Photos must not be used as repeated decoration on text pages. If a photo enters, it needs to teach something: light, composition, product, gesture, texture, context, behavior, grid or application. To sustain analytical pages without photo, use hierarchy, scale, lines, numbers and composition. Outline/solid icons, swatches and micro-diagrams only enter when they directly explain that page's content.

Before presenting references, the Maestro needs to identify failures. Any image that looks like an error, platform noise, undue external asset, placeholder, loose ad, textual screenshot, image without clear relation to the brand or reference simulation must be discarded from the PDF. The discard must be recorded in the inventory, but not presented as a valid reference.

Real extracted photos must appear in volume proportional to the archive. If the site or Instagram delivers dozens of valid photos, the PDF can't show only a small board and look shallow. The correct response is bento grid, mosaic or sequence of editorial boards, preserving proportion and full reading of the image. Don't cut product, face, logo, packaging, typography or gesture for template convenience. Visual repetitions of the same asset must be deduplicated to open space for real variety. Banners, cards, textual screenshots and noise don't enter this gallery; they enter only as visual assets when useful to explain application.

Bento grid needs to adapt composition to the real number of images. A board with 3 photos can't use the same grid of 9 and leave the page looking empty. Last boards must redistribute scale, position and useful area to look intentional.

Technique name isn't a title for the client. Don't call a page "Photo bento", "Grid", "Template" or "Module". The title must sound human and clear: "Photo gallery", "Photos analyzed", "Visual board", "Light study", "Photographic rhythm" or another name that explains the page's function.

The Maestro must:

- preserve proportion by default;
- crop only when there's clear aesthetic intent;
- never mutilate product, face, lettering, packaging or relevant information;
- alternate hero image pages, mosaic, detail and board;
- create breathing between image and text;
- contextualize what the image teaches;
- separate logo from visual gallery, unless the page is about visual signature;
- avoid generic frames when the image itself can breathe against the background.

## Typography

Typography must be found, interpreted and used as identity.

Priority:

1. Font sent by the user;
2. Font detected on site, CSS, PDF, deck or image;
3. Similar font installed locally;
4. Coherent editorial fallback, with justification.

The PDF must use real typographic scale:

- display for cover, chapter and big numbers;
- body for reading;
- mono or metadata for inventory, paths and technical notes;
- weights and sizes with perceptible contrast.

Minimum reading rules:

- Body text must have a comfortable line, never occupying the page's full width for long reading.
- Running text should aim for 45 to 75 characters per line.
- Body must have comfortable line-height, usually between 1.3 and 1.45.
- Large titles can have tighter line-height, but can never collide.
- Kicker and label in uppercase need letter-spacing.
- Don't use more than three font weights without a clear reason.
- Title, subtitle, body and caption must look like different levels even in thumbnail.

## Colors and tables

Whenever a hex appears on a page, it must come with a visual color sample.

Tables can't look like raw spreadsheets. They must be treated as editorial matrices:

- strong header;
- rows with breathing;
- subtle zebra when it improves reading;
- color samples inside cells when there's hex;
- clear hierarchy between title, column, value and explanation;
- thin lines, not heavy grids;
- width thought for reading, not for data dump.

Borders and separators can never cross, touch or sit above the text. Every cell must have real internal padding, height proportional to content and separator line positioned below the text block. If a cell breaks into two or more lines, the whole row grows together. Table with border invading text is rejected, even if the content is correct.

The palette can't treat all colors as equal. The Maestro must differentiate dominant color, background, title, text, CTA, CTA text, accent and rare colors. When there's sufficient material, it must estimate percentage of visual presence and make the basis of the analysis clear.

Text blocks need size adapted to the content. Text overflowing the block is forbidden. Huge block with small text lost inside is also forbidden. The layout must choose between smaller card, larger card, column, list or new page.

## Content → layout decision map

The page type is born from the content. The Maestro must choose the form by what needs to be understood:

- Short, decisive insight → thesis page or big data.
- List of practical rules → numbered list, cards or do/don't page.
- Parallel concepts → editorial matrix.
- Palette → swatches with function, percentage, usage and common error.
- Typography → typographic specimen by function: title, text, button, caption and metadata.
- Photography → gallery, visual board, hero, enlarged detail or light study.
- Comparison → before/after, approved/forbidden or 2×2 axis.
- Process or sequence → timeline.
- Applications → touchpoints map or context grid.
- Argumentative text → two columns or editorial synthesis.

If a page looks empty, maybe the content should be grouped. If it looks crammed, maybe the content is asking for another structure. The solution isn't to reduce font until it fits.

## Brands without ready visual material

When the brand doesn't have site, social network, portfolio or enough images, the PDF must not pretend it analyzed what doesn't exist. It also can't be poor.

In these cases, the visual direction is born from three sources:

- guided answers from the person;
- aspirational references and anti-references collected;
- visual hypotheses proposed by the Maestro and validated before becoming a rule.

The document must continue covering the same intentions: palette, typography, photography, light, composition, texture, grid, margins, tone of voice, vocabulary, applications and limits. The difference is the origin of the evidence. The PDF must indicate when something is observed, declared or proposed, without technical language and without looking like a gap report.

## Visual QA before delivering

Before delivering, the Maestro must visually review the PDF or exported previews.

Whenever possible, the PDF must be rasterized or opened in preview before delivery. Visual validation needs to happen looking at real pages, not just reading the source file.

Mandatory checklist:

- no page looks like markdown;
- no page has crammed text;
- no list became a running line;
- important images weren't cut badly;
- there's contrast between title, body, note and caption;
- there's consistent margin;
- there's breathing between image and text;
- the pages look like a presentation, not a report;
- the visual repertoire appears throughout the document, not just at the beginning;
- photos aren't repeated as decoration;
- icons, swatches and graphic details only appear when they make sense for the page;
- there's no decorative side column repeated without direct relation to the content;
- text-only pages are the exception and must have strong composition;
- the aesthetic looks intentional, not generic;
- the PDF is presentable to client, agency, designer and creative direction.
- cover, gallery, palette, typography, matrix and dense pages were seen in preview;
- no text starts with lowercase because of automatic line break;
- no title uses internal technique term, like "bento", "grid" or "template";
- no hexadecimal appears without visual color sample;
- no block looks like it was filled on top of a fixed template.

If it fails, render again.
