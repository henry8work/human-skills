## 0. Doctrine

The biggest strategic weakness of average Brazilian brands is a generic audience. "Women 25-45 interested in quality of life" — a description that fits 80 million people and directs absolutely nothing. A brand with generic audience produces generic copy, generic visual, generic positioning — because it doesn't know whom it's talking to.

The difference between mediocre brand and remembered brand lies in **audience specificity**. Patagonia talks to specific people who climb real mountains, not with "nature lovers". Aesop talks to specific people who value apothecary ritual, not with "premium cosmetic consumer". Stripe talks to specific developer-founder in early-stage, not with "technology company".

Every decision in this document starts from a premise: **persona is fictional construction based on real data**. It's not statistics (demography), it's not fantasy (commercial avatar). It's a dense character built from real qualitative research, which guides coherent creative decisions.

The right question isn't "who is our audience?". It's "which specific person do we take as protagonist of every creative decision?". When the team says "would Mariana understand this text?" it works because Mariana is specific. "Would the target audience understand?" doesn't work because audience is an abstraction that decides nothing.

## 2. The 12 founding principles

### Principle 1 — Persona is construction, not research

Persona isn't "average customer profile" extracted from a spreadsheet. It's a **dense fictional character** built from qualitative research data, which guides coherent creative decisions.

The difference between useful persona and useless persona lies in narrative density. Useless persona: "Woman 30-45, class B, likes quality". Useful persona: "Mariana, 32, wedding photographer in Porto Alegre, lives with her boyfriend and two dogs, bills R$ 24k/month, works on a custom-made carpentry desk, listens to Café da Manhã podcast from Folha in the car, reads 1 book per month, does pilates 2x/week, sleeps at 11pm."

Density generates decision. Generality generates improvisation.

### Principle 2 — Psychography > demography

Demography (age, gender, income, geography) describes but doesn't decide. Two 32-year-old women, class B, in Porto Alegre can have opposite worldviews. Demography is a statistical coordinate; behavior is driven by values, fears, aspirations, contexts.

Indi Young: deep qualitative research extracts "mental models" — how the person thinks, decides, feels about the relevant domain. Mental model is the real unit of segmentation.

### Principle 3 — Real behavior > declared opinion

"What would you buy?" is a useless question — people don't know what they would buy. "What did you buy last week? Why?" is a valuable question — behavior already happened, motive is real reconstruction.

Rob Fitzpatrick (*The Mom Test*): every mother says her business idea is good. Real validation requires questions that don't give a chance to validate — only to reveal.

### Principle 4 — JTBD: customer hires product to do a job

Clayton Christensen: customer doesn't buy product. Hires product to do a job (functional + emotional + social). The same customer hires different products for the same job in different contexts.

Classic case: McDonald's milkshake is hired to "occupy 30min of morning traffic + sate lightly + don't mess up car". That's the real job. Optimizing for that job = increase straw, thicken shake (lasts longer), make it easy to hold with 1 hand.

The same brand could sell a totally different milkshake to a child in the late afternoon (job: gift + fun + play with mom). Same product, distinct jobs.

### Principle 5 — Functional + identity + social aspiration

3 layers of motivation (Christensen, Young, Cialdini, Eugene Wei):

- **Functional** — what they want to **do** (practical result)
- **Emotional** — how they want to **feel**
- **Social/identity** — how they want to be **perceived** by others

A strong brand answers all 3. A weak brand answers only functional. "Buy my course and learn X" is pure functional. "Buy my course, learn X (functional), feel prepared for the next level (emotional), be seen as authority in the niche (social)" is complete.

Eugene Wei (*Status as a Service*): social platforms (Instagram, LinkedIn, Twitter) operate primarily in status — identity + social signaling. A brand that ignores this layer loses participation in the conversation.

### Principle 6 — Anti-persona defines better than persona

The most revealing question: **whom do you NOT serve, even if they pay?**

A specific anti-persona protects:
- Team (from the customer who always causes problems)
- Brand (from de-characterization)
- Culture (from erosion through exception)
- Positioning (from dilution)

A brand without anti-persona accepts everyone. A brand with anti-persona refuses quickly, keeps focus, grows in the right direction.

### Principle 7 — Canonical weekly decision is more useful than biography

A persona with a generic biography guides little. A persona with a **canonical weekly decision** guides everything:

> "Every Friday morning, she decides which content from the next batch she'll produce over the weekend — usually between 'carousel about composition in photography' (which she thinks no one sees) or 'story of behind-the-scenes of the wedding she covered' (which has more views but doesn't build authority). This tension between reach and authority is where she always gets stuck."

From that canonical decision, the brand extracts: which piece to produce, which tone to use, which CTA to offer, which value to add. Concrete decision generates concrete strategy.

### Principle 8 — Audience's own vocabulary > brand's internal vocabulary

Joanna Wiebe (Copyhackers): most persuasive copy is the one that **uses the customers' exact words**, not marketing words.

Method: collecting interview transcripts, reviews, comments, DMs. Mapping textual phrases that repeat. Substituting internal copy with the audience's real language.

A brand that says "innovative time management solutions" loses to a brand that says "I'm drowning in tasks, I can't handle it" — the second uses the audience's own words.

### Principle 9 — Light buyer matters more than heavy buyer (Sharp)

Byron Sharp (Ehrenberg-Bass): brand growth comes from **adding light buyers** (people who bought 1-2 times), not from **retaining heavy buyers** (already bought a lot).

Counterintuitive implication: a brand doesn't grow by focusing on "super-fans". It grows by being memorable and available to the **mass of occasional buyers**.

Strategic persona is usually archetypal light buyer — person who knows brand, considers, buys sporadically. Not fanbase.

### Principle 10 — Mental availability + Physical availability (Sharp)

Brand growth = sum of:

- **Mental availability** — how easily the brand comes to mind when consumer thinks of the category
- **Physical availability** — how easy it is to find/buy when it comes to mind

Strategic persona considers BOTH. A brand with brilliant positioning but bad distribution doesn't grow. A brand with wide distribution but weak memory neither.

### Principle 11 — Persona evolves

Audience changes. Technology changes habits (TikTok changed video consumption). Generation changes values (Gen Z differs from Millennial in sustainability, status, work). Cultural crisis changes behavior (pandemic permanently changed home office).

A persona created in 2020 is outdated in 2026. Mandatory annual calibration. Ideally: continuous qualitative research (quarterly interviews with 5-10 real customers).

### Principle 12 — Persona must fit in the team's head

A persona with a 30-page document no one remembers. A persona with a proper name + 1 dense paragraph + 3 canonical attributes fits in the head of the whole team.

Test: ask designer, copy, salesperson "who is Mariana?". If they answer coherently, the persona works. If they hesitate or diverge, the persona is too abstract.

## 3. Consolidated frameworks (with attribution)

### 3.1 — Cooper Personas (Alan Cooper, 1998+)

Persona as a software design tool:

- **Proper name** + photo + biography
- **Goals** (what they want to achieve)
- **Behaviors** (how they act)
- **Context** (in what situation they encounter brand/product)
- **Pain points** (what frustrates)

Cooper distinguishes:
- **Primary persona** — design optimized for them
- **Secondary persona** — cannot be ignored
- **Anti-persona** — do not design for them

### 3.2 — Mental Models (Indi Young, 2008)

Deep qualitative research on **how the audience thinks** about the domain:

- Listening sessions (1-2h per participant)
- Mapping of tasks, emotions, philosophies
- Grouping into "towers" (groups of similar thought)
- Comparison with offered features

Useful for identifying gaps between what the brand offers and what the audience mentally seeks.

### 3.3 — Jobs-to-be-Done (Christensen + Moesta + Ulwick)

Structure:

```
When [context/situation],
I want [functional job],
so I can [emotional + identity/social job].
```

Example (reference):
> "When I'm on Friday morning deciding what to produce over the weekend, I want to have a system that organizes my ideas and produces the heavy visual material for me, so I can use my time on what only I can do (writing the copy with my voice, choosing the photo that has soul, recording the audio)."

JTBD replaces demography as base of segmentation. Same person has different jobs in different contexts.

### 3.4 — Forces of progress (Bob Moesta)

In every purchase decision, 4 forces operate:

- **Push** (current pain) — "I'm unhappy with X"
- **Pull** (attraction of the new solution) — "this thing seems to solve it"
- **Anxiety** (fear of the new) — "what if it doesn't work"
- **Habit** (attachment to status quo) — "the current way I already know"

For customer to progress: Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit.

Effective brand increases Push (shows pain) + Pull (shows solution) AND decreases Anxiety (guarantee, proof) + Habit (eases transition).

### 3.5 — Influence (Cialdini, 7 principles)

7 universal principles of persuasion:

1. **Reciprocity** — person returns favor received
2. **Commitment/consistency** — person wants to be consistent with public statement
3. **Social proof** — person imitates what others do
4. **Authority** — person follows recognizable authority figure
5. **Liking** — person says yes to whom they like
6. **Scarcity** — person values what is rare
7. **Unity** (added in 2016) — person identifies with tribes

Apply with ethics: each principle can be used to manipulate or to facilitate the right decision. The difference is information honesty.

### 3.6 — Fogg Behavior Model (BJ Fogg)

Behavior occurs when 3 elements converge:

```
B = MAP

Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt
```

- **Motivation** — how motivated the person is
- **Ability** — how easy it is to do
- **Prompt** — trigger that fires the action

To change behavior:
- Increase Motivation (expensive, slow)
- Increase Ability (make it easier — more effective)
- Trigger Prompt at the right moment (most effective)

A brand that wants to be bought should focus on **reducing friction** (Ability) + **being present at the moment of decision** (Prompt) — not on "creating desire" (Motivation, slow).

### 3.7 — Ehrenberg-Bass (Byron Sharp)

Empirical principles based on decades of FMCG data:

1. **Penetration** > Loyalty (more buyers > loyal buyers)
2. **Mental availability** + **Physical availability**
3. **Distinctive Brand Assets** (consistent colors, sounds, symbols)
4. **Light buyers** matter more than heavy buyers
5. **Mass marketing** works; narrowcasting rarely

### 3.8 — Status as a Service (Eugene Wei)

Social platforms operate as status systems. 2 variables:

- **Utility** — practical utility (information, connection, function)
- **Social capital** — status signaled when using

Winning platforms maximize social capital with low friction of use.

Implication for brand: communication in social network needs to **give opportunity for identity signaling** to the user (share / comment / like = "this represents me"). "Pure informational" content without identity component has low propagation.

### 3.9 — The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)

How to conduct discovery interview without bias:

3 rules:
1. **Talk about their life, not your idea** — no pitch of the idea
2. **Ask about specifics in past, not generics or opinions about future** — real past, not opinion about future
3. **Talk less, listen more** — listen > talk

Right questions:
- ✅ "Tell me about the last time you did X. How did it go?"
- ✅ "Why did you do it that way?"
- ✅ "What did you try before?"

Wrong questions:
- ❌ "Would you use a product that does X?"
- ❌ "How much would you pay for this?"
- ❌ "Do you like my idea?"

## 4. Anatomy of dense persona

### 4.1 — Mandatory components

Every strategic persona has:

**Identification:**
- Proper name (fictional)
- Photo (real, not avatar — can be illustration if careful)
- Exact age (not range)
- Concrete profession (not category)
- Named city (not state)

**Life context:**
- Family composition (whom they live with)
- Approximate income range
- Academic background
- Hobbies/cultural practices
- Typical weekly routine
- Digital habits (apps, sites, communities)

**Mental model:**
- What they value (3-5 values)
- What they fear (professional + identity)
- What they aspire to (functional + identity)
- View of the category (how they think about the brand's domain)

**Behavior:**
- Canonical weekly decision (Principle 7)
- Typical touchpoints (where they encounter brand/category)
- Typical conversion sequence (from unawareness to purchase)

**Vocabulary:**
- Words she herself uses (her jargon)
- Words she rejects or mocks (generic marketing jargon)

**Cultural references:**
- What media she consumes (books, podcasts, series, accounts she follows)
- What brands she already buys (in sector + outside)

### 4.2 — Narrative density

A narrative persona reads like a contemporary novel character. Not like a market research slide:

> ✅ Dense: "Mariana, 32, wedding photographer in Porto Alegre. Lives in a 60m² apartment in Bom Fim with boyfriend Leandro (freelance designer) and two dogs (Olha and Bento). Billed R$ 24k in January, average billing R$ 18-25k varying with seasonality. Graduated in Social Communication from UFRGS, took 3 online photography courses afterward (one from Renato Stockler on Domestika). Works on a custom-made carpentry desk that she and Leandro built in 2023. Equipment: Sony A7 IV + Sigma 35mm Art + Godox AD200 flash. Digital tools: Lightroom (essential), Capture One (but only used in the course), Notion (organization), Instagram (single channel, 14k followers), Pinterest (refs), Are.na (professional refs). Saturday morning she goes to a gallery (Fundação Iberê has a new exhibition; she always goes). Listens to podcast in the car, mainly Café da Manhã (Folha) and loose episodes of Roda de Conversa (Zé Antunes). Reads 1 book per month (currently 'Memórias Póstumas de Brás Cubas' — re-reading). No TV, subscribes to MUBI and Apple TV+. Wakes at 7:30am, sleeps at 11pm, does pilates 2x/week."

> ❌ Generic: "Woman 25-45, class B, with interest in professional photography, values quality and organization."

Density generates decision. Generality generates improvisation.

### 4.3 — Tier 1 vs Tier 2 personas

- **Tier 1 (Primary persona)** — brand's protagonist. Every creative decision optimized for her. Maximum density.
- **Tier 2 (Secondary persona)** — second significant profile. Less density, but mapped. Criterion: represents 20%+ of revenue or audience.

More than 2 personas dilutes focus. Small brand: 1 primary + 0-1 secondary. Enterprise brand: 1 primary per product segment.

### 4.4 — Calibration with real audience

Persona is construction. But it needs to be **periodically calibrated with real audience** so as not to become disconnected fantasy.

Calibration methods:

| Method | Frequency | Output |
|---|---|---|
| In-depth interviews (5 customers) | Semi-annual | Confirmation/refinement of fears, aspirations, vocabulary |
| Digital behavior analysis (analytics, hotjar, Search Console) | Monthly | Real touchpoints, real decisions |
| Comment caption analysis | Quarterly | Real vocabulary |
| Quantitative research (Survey) | Annual | Validation of hypotheses |

## 5. Anti-persona — whom you refuse

### 5.1 — Definition

A specific and legitimate person who comes looking for you but is NOT the right customer. Concrete details, not caricature.

> ✅ Specific anti-persona: "B2B startup founder (usually SaaS) who discovered the brand via case at an event, comes with expectation of 'I need a creative agency to set up our visual identity'. Will appear with a half-baked brief, will ask for discount ('it's just to start'), will pressure 2-3 week deadline for deliverable that would take 6, and will disappear during feedback. This profile always causes problems in delivery and never becomes a publishable case."

> ❌ Vague anti-persona: "Annoying customer."

### 5.2 — Why refuse (justification)

A declared anti-persona needs concrete reason:

- Economy doesn't close (expensive to serve)
- Delivery will suffer (impossible brief)
- Brand result burns (bad case)
- Internal culture suffers (toxic customer)

Anti-persona "because we don't like them" is weakness. Anti-persona "because [defensible business reason]" is strategy.

### 5.3 — How to refuse (script)

Honest, respectful refusal, without closing the door in case the person matures:

> "From what you described, I don't think we're the best partner for this moment — I recommend talking to [alternative A or B], who serve profiles with a more open brief. If later on the brief matures and you want to talk again, we're available."

### 5.4 — Warning signs in initial conversation

3-5 phrases/behaviors that indicate anti-persona, even if the person seems interesting at first contact:

- "It's just to start, then we'll see better pricing later"
- "I need it for next week"
- "I can't explain what I want, but I'll know when I see"
- "Can you send me 3 options to choose from?"
- "I had already started work with [another vendor], but it didn't work out"

Recognizing early avoids problems later.

## 6. Jobs-to-be-Done — behavior as starting point

### 6.1 — JTBD structure

```
When [situation/context],
I want [functional result],
so I can [emotional + social/identity result].
```

Example (classic Christensen case — McDonald's milkshake):
> "When I'm in morning traffic with 30min to work, I want a liquid snack that lasts the entire trip and distracts me, so I can arrive at work full and without having made a mess in the car."

### 6.2 — Same customer, distinct jobs

The same person hires different products for the same job in different contexts. And the same product serves distinct jobs in distinct contexts.

McDonald's milkshake:
- Morning job: occupy trip + sate lightly + no mess
- Afternoon job: gift + fun + play with child

Same person, same product, two jobs. Which job does the brand optimize for? Strategic decision.

### 6.3 — How to discover real jobs

Qualitative interviews focused on **last real purchase** (not abstract opinion):

- "Tell me about the last time you bought X. How did it go?"
- "What did you do to solve this problem before? Why did you stop?"
- "When specifically did you decide to buy?"
- "What happened in your life the day/week before the purchase?"

Jobs emerge from reconstruction of real moment, not from abstract question about future.

### 6.4 — Applied forces of progress

In every decision, map:

- **Push** (current pain) — what was bad that motivated change?
- **Pull** (attraction) — what did this solution seem to offer?
- **Anxiety** (fear) — what was scary about changing?
- **Habit** (status quo) — what was easy to maintain?

Effective brand increases Push + Pull, decreases Anxiety + Habit. Communication adjusts to these 4 forces.

## 7. Mental availability + Behavior change

### 7.1 — Mental availability (Byron Sharp)

Brand grows by being **remembered at the right time**. When consumer thinks of the category, brand needs to come to mind in seconds.

Building mental availability:
- Distinctive Brand Assets (consistent colors, sounds, symbols over decades)
- Wide reach (more people exposed, with frequency)
- Continuity (don't interrupt presence)
- Variety of touchpoints (various situations in which brand appears)

Small brand usually errs by wanting "deep engagement with niche". Sharp shows: grows more by being **lightly remembered by many** than deeply engaged by few.

### 7.2 — Behavior change (Fogg, Eyal, Duhigg)

Changing customer behavior = engineering, not inspiration.

Fogg model (B = MAP):
- **Motivation** — high at moment of decision? (often low)
- **Ability** — how easy to do? (reduce friction)
- **Prompt** — trigger at right moment? (notification, contextual reminder)

Effective brand reduces friction (Ability) and creates contextual prompts. Doesn't try to "motivate" (expensive, slow, often fails).

### 7.3 — Habit vs conscious decision

Kahneman: 95% of daily decisions are System 1 (automatic, habit). Only 5% System 2 (deliberative).

A brand bought by habit (color + shelf position + mental recognition) doesn't require conscious decision. A brand that requires conscious decision loses to the first the brain recognizes.

Implication: distinctive brand assets > differentiation messaging. Brand color, symbol, position in category matter more than slogan or benefit statement.

## 8. GOOD vs BAD audience (canonical cases)

### 8.1 — GOOD audience (to study)

**Brands with clear strategic persona:**

- **Patagonia** — serves specifically "outdoor enthusiast with environmental consciousness". Refuses mass-market even if it pays.
- **Apple** — "creative professional + aspirational consumer" defined since 1997. Consistently refuses low-end market.
- **Stripe** — "early-stage developer-founder". Everything in product and marketing speaks to this specific profile.
- **Aesop** — "cultured urban consumer who values apothecary ritual". Physical store, vocabulary, packaging — all speak to her.
- **Liquid Death** — "Gen Z + Millennial irreverent who hates generic wellness lifestyle". Grew from zero to $700M without traditional media.
- **Nubank** — "Brazilian millennial frustrated with traditional bank". Human language ("disposition to be loved"), direct communication. Largest LATAM fintech IPO.

**Common characteristics:**
- Specific and dense persona (not demography)
- Declared anti-persona (market refusal)
- Audience's own vocabulary (not marketing jargon)
- Touchpoints where audience effectively is
- Clear jobs (functional + emotional + social)

### 8.2 — BAD audience (to avoid)

**Saturated anti-patterns:**

- "Everything to everyone" — no defined persona
- Empty demographic persona ("women 25-45 class B")
- Aspirational persona disconnected from reality ("billionaire sustainable conscious CEO woman")
- Multiple personas (5+) trying to serve everyone
- Idealized "super-fan" persona that doesn't exist in reality
- Non-existent anti-persona (accepts any customer)

**Brazilian cases to study as how NOT to do** (anonymous):
- Personal coach brands with persona "everyone who wants to grow"
- Traditional banks trying to talk to "all Brazilians"
- Fashion e-commerce that segments by broad demography without psychography

## 9. Audience that SELLS vs REPELS

### 9.1 — SELLS

- Specific persona + clear JTBD = persuasive copy
- Audience vocabulary used literally (VOC copy)
- Social proof with real people (not anonymous)
- Implicitly declared anti-persona ("this is for X, not Y") — attracts X, repels Y, both valid
- Touchpoints where audience already is (not where brand wants to be)
- Communication aligned with audience cultural references

### 9.2 — REPELS

- Generic persona = generic copy = doesn't connect
- Brand's internal vocabulary (technical jargon, "solutions", "ecosystem")
- Anonymous social proof ("Maria, satisfied customer")
- Trying to please everyone = convinces no one
- Communication in wrong channels ("let's go to TikTok!" without persona being there)
- Wrong cultural references (irony in sincere audience; sincerity in ironic audience)

### 9.3 — Central commercial principle

Specific audience + calibrated communication = high conversion + low acquisition cost. Generic audience + generic communication = low conversion + high acquisition cost.

Small brand that defines specific audience surpasses big brand trying to serve everyone — in conversion and in margin.

## 10. Audience that AGES WELL vs DATES FAST

### 10.1 — AGES WELL

- Persona anchored in **universal human value** (safety, connection, creativity, autonomy)
- JTBD related to **fundamental need** (not trend)
- Behavior mapped in **stable routine** (not fad)

### 10.2 — DATES FAST

- Persona defined by generation-moment (Gen Z 2024 vs Gen Z 2027 are different)
- Persona defined by platform (TikTok-only persona becomes problem if TikTok declines)
- Persona defined by specific cultural trend ("BeReal user" aged in 18 months)
- Persona defined by specific tech ("AI early adopter") — category changes fast

### 10.3 — Principle of longevity

Strategic persona is anchored in **deep psychography + functional JTBD**. Surface behavior (which app they use, which jargon they speak) changes. Values, fears, aspirations change less.

Continuous calibration is necessary — but the persona's core should survive 5-10 years.

## 11. Research and persona anti-patterns

### 11.1 — Persona

- ❌ Demography disguised as persona ("Woman 30-45, class B")
- ❌ Persona invented without real qualitative research
- ❌ Disconnected aspirational persona (marketing avatar)
- ❌ 5+ personas trying to serve everyone
- ❌ Persona without proper name (anonymous)
- ❌ Persona without canonical weekly decision
- ❌ Persona without corresponding anti-persona
- ❌ "Tokenized diverse" persona (mandatory one of each ethnicity/orientation)

### 11.2 — Research

- ❌ Question about future ("would you buy?")
- ❌ Validation question ("do you like?")
- ❌ Leading question ("don't you think this is great?")
- ❌ Quantitative survey before qualitative (measures without understanding)
- ❌ Research only with existing customers (survivorship bias)
- ❌ Single-source research (one source is not enough)
- ❌ Drawing conclusion from N=5 without statistical care

### 11.3 — Application

- ❌ Persona created once, never calibrated
- ❌ Shelved persona (no one on team remembers)
- ❌ Generic communication while persona is specific (disconnect)
- ❌ Accepting every customer despite declared anti-persona
- ❌ Changing persona at every launch

## 12. Qualitative and quantitative research methods

### 12.1 — Deep qualitative interview (1-2h per person)

**When to use:** initial discovery, semi-annual calibration, hypothesis validation.

**How to do (Mom Test + Indi Young):**
1. Sample: 5-12 representative people
2. Location: their environment (home, office) or deep call
3. Semi-structured script: real past behavior
4. Recording + transcript (with consent)
5. Analysis: patterns + textual quotes

**Output:** mental models, JTBD, own vocabulary.

### 12.2 — Listening sessions (Indi Young)

**When to use:** understand mental model of domain.

**How to do:**
1. No script — person talks about broad topic
2. Researcher listens + does minimal follow-up
3. 60-90min per session
4. Analysis: mapping of thought "towers"

**Output:** audience's thought structure.

### 12.3 — Digital behavior analysis

**When to use:** validation of real touchpoints, real decisions.

**Tools:**
- Google Analytics / GA4
- Hotjar / Microsoft Clarity (heatmaps + recordings)
- Search Console (real queries)
- Social platforms analytics

**Output:** real behavior (not declared).

### 12.4 — Caption and comment analysis

**When to use:** capture the audience's real vocabulary.

**How to do:**
1. Collect 100+ comments and DMs
2. Identify textual phrases that repeat
3. Map emotions, pains, vocabulary
4. Substitute internal copy with real language (VOC copy)

### 12.5 — Quantitative research (Survey)

**When to use:** hypothesis validation, market size estimation.

**Principles:**
- Quali first (to have the right questions)
- Relevant sample (not friends, not own LinkedIn)
- Neutral questions (not leading)
- Sufficient N for significance

**Output:** quantitative validation of qualitative patterns.

### 12.6 — NPS and relational metrics

**Classic NPS question:**
> "From 0 to 10, how much would you recommend [brand] to a friend or colleague?"

- 0-6: detractors
- 7-8: passives
- 9-10: promoters

NPS = % promoters - % detractors

**Limitation:** NPS is a vague metric. The follow-up question "why?" is where real value lives.

## 13. Quality criteria

### 13.1 — The 7 criteria for persona

1. **Specific** — proper name, exact age, concrete profession, named city
2. **Dense** — rich biography, life context, detailed digital habits
3. **Psychographic** — values, fears, aspirations, mental model
4. **Calibrated** — based on real qualitative research, not invention
5. **Actionable** — guides concrete creative decision
6. **Memorable** — team remembers and cites ("would Mariana do this?")
7. **Declared anti-persona** — whom you refuse, why

5+ "yes" = competent persona. 7/7 = excellent persona.

### 13.2 — The "would Mariana do it?" test

A useful persona is cited in decisions. Question: in the last 4 weeks, how many times has someone on the team said "would [persona] do this?" or "would [persona] understand this text?"?

If never, persona is decoration. If weekly, persona is alive.

### 13.3 — The refusal test

A specific persona generates refusal of opportunities outside the target. A brand with clear persona refuses generic briefs quickly.

Question: in the last 4 weeks, how many opportunities have you explicitly refused because "doesn't fit our persona"?

If zero, persona is vague.
