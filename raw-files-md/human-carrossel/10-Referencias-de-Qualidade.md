## Why this page exists

These are two complete carousels as **editorial quality anchors**. When R2 generates copy, it compares its output against these examples. If the result doesn't match the same level of density, angle and tone — it redoes it.

They are not templates. They are the **minimum acceptable quality standard**. The real carousel can be better; never worse.

## Example 1 — Carousel about content creation

**Input:** Analysis of 1,168 posts showing that marketing/branding has a 7.1% hit rate, while generational topics have 1 in 3.

**Axis:** Market · **Funnel:** Top (reach new people)

### Triage

| Field | Excerpt |
|-------|---------|
| Transformation | Creators keep posting about the technical niche thinking it drives reach — but the data shows the opposite: the more technical, the less viral. The worst topic to go viral is exactly the one you master the most. |
| Central friction | The person knows the subject but doesn't know the audience. They confuse "what I know" with "what people want to see". Result: real authority, zero reach. |
| Dominant narrative angle | Data that contradicts intuition — the topic you avoid performs better than the one you choose. |
| Evidence | A) Marketing/branding: 7.1% hit rate. B) Generations/behavior: 1 in 3 posts go viral — 4x more. C) Nostalgia: ~28% without technical expertise. |

### Chosen headline (option 3 of 10)

```
Why the topic you master the most
is the one that goes viral least on Instagram.
```

**Pattern:** Adapted Generational Question · **Triggers:** Curiosity + Identity

### Narrative architecture

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| Hook | You studied your subject for years. You have cases, you have results, you have authority. And even so: 13 likes, 2 comments, zero reach. The problem isn't the content — it's the topic. |
| Mechanism | Instagram does not distribute content by technical quality. It distributes by engagement speed in the first hours. And engagement comes from emotional identification — not expertise. Who identifies with "Gen Z stopped going to parties"? Half of Brazil. Who identifies with "4 CAC strategies for B2B SaaS"? Two hundred people. |
| Proof | A) Across 1,168 posts, marketing and branding had a 7.1% hit rate. B) Posts about generations went viral 1 in 3. C) Nostalgia and behavior: ~28% hit rate. |
| Application | This does not mean abandoning the niche. It means using it as a destination, not a starting point. The cover surfs the trending topic. The middle delivers the specialty. The nutritionist who makes a carousel about "why everyone is running" and ends with nutrition for runners has reach and authority at the same time. |
| Direction | The next carousel: start from the topic, not the content. Pick a subject that's trending in Brazil — generational, cultural, behavioral — and find the angle that connects with what you do. |

## Example 2 — Carousel about a cultural phenomenon

**Input:** Trend of creators using running as the topic of viral posts.

**Axis:** Culture · **Funnel:** Top

### Triage

| Field | Excerpt |
|-------|---------|
| Transformation | Running stopped being an endurance sport and became a symbol of urban identity. Whoever runs is not training — they are declaring values: discipline, mental health, belonging to a tribe. |
| Central friction | A historically solitary sport with no audience became the center of one of the largest content communities in Brazil — and no one knows exactly how to explain why. |
| Dominant narrative angle | Cultural phenomenon: what running reveals about who we are in 2026. |
| Evidence | A) Posts about running: 10.6k average likes. B) The Brazilian running market grew 34% in 2024. C) Running clubs in SP multiplied 5x in 3 years. |

### Chosen headline (option 7 of 10)

```
Investigating why running became
the favorite drug of anxious adults.
```

**Pattern:** Investigating + Brand/Phenomenon · **Triggers:** Curiosity + Identity + Indignation

### Copy of the 7 slides (adapted version)

**Slide 1 — Cover**

Headline: `INVESTIGATING WHY RUNNING BECAME THE FAVORITE DRUG OF ANXIOUS ADULTS`

**Slide 2 — Hook (Dark)**

Tag: `THE PHENOMENON`

text 3: In 2020, gyms closed and everyone went running. Except the gyms opened again — and people **did not stop**.

text 4: 34% more Brazilians run than two years ago. It's not a fitness trend. It's a cultural phenomenon that needs explaining.

**Slide 3 — Mechanism (Gradient)**

Tag: `WHY IT WORKS`

→ **Productive solitude** — time without notification, without a meeting, without a deliverable

→ **Visible result** — you literally see where you got to today vs. yesterday

**Slide 4 — Proof (Dark)**

Tag: `THE NUMBERS`

**Slide 6 — Direction (Dark)**

## Quality patterns extracted from these examples

Use as a checklist before approving any carousel:

**Copy:**
- Each slide has at most 2 text blocks — never 3
- Block 1 contextualizes. Block 2 deepens or contradicts
- Data always with number + source + year
- Never finish a slide with a closed statement — always leave an opening for the next
- Slides 7-8 narratively prepare the CTA — never jump straight to "comment X"

**Headline:**
- Passes the rejection checklist before reaching the final selection
- At least 2 identifiable emotional triggers
- Uses at least 1 positive-lift pattern
- Doesn't use any forbidden words

**Structure:**
- Triage done before writing any slide
- Narrative architecture validated before render
- Slide 2's hook connects directly with the cover headline
- The CTA connects with the topic, is short and keeps the logo/brand name in the spotlight

## How R2 uses this page

Before approving the carousel's copy for render, R2:

1. Reads this entire page
2. Compares the output against the two examples:
   - Is the block density equivalent?
   - Is the angle as specific?
   - Does the hook have the same tension strength?
   - Does the closing pivot or just summarize?
3. If the output is below these examples → redoes the copy
4. Only proceeds to render when it's at the same level
