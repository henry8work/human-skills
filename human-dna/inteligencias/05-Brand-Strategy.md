## 0. Doctrine

Brand strategy is the first layer of DNA. Everything (voice, visual, photography, behavior) is a manifestation of strategy. A brand without strategy has inconsistent voice, oscillating visual, erratic behavior — because every decision is improvisation, with no foundation.

The biggest difference between a lasting brand and a passing brand is strategy. Apple survived multiple repositionings because the purpose ("technology meets liberal arts") remained. Patagonia weathered environmental crises because the core ("we're in business to save our home planet") never gave in. Brands that come and go usually lack a strategic foundation — they were trend, not thesis.

Every decision in this document starts from a premise: **brand strategy is a centuries-old practice** with a consolidated canon. From Ogilvy in the 50s to Aaker in the 80s to Neumeier in the 2000s — the discipline matured. Whoever improvises today repeats mistakes the literature corrected decades ago.

The function of strategy is to turn **hard decision into obvious decision**. A brand with a clear strategy refuses a generic brief in 5 seconds ("doesn't fit our positioning"). A brand without strategy spends weeks debating every opportunity.

## 2. The 12 founding principles

### Principle 1 — Strategy is choice, not enumeration

Marty Neumeier: *"A brand is a person's gut feeling about a product, service, or organization."* Strategy defines which gut feeling you want to provoke — and which one you do NOT.

The biggest Brazilian strategic weakness: brands that try to "be everything to everyone". Result: nothing to no one. Strong strategy refuses segments, refuses messages, refuses opportunities.

Roger Martin (*Playing to Win*): strategy has 5 inseparable choices:
1. Winning aspiration
2. Where to play
3. How to win
4. Capabilities needed
5. Management systems

A brand that can't articulate these 5 doesn't have strategy — it has operation.

### Principle 2 — Positioning is mind-share, not market-share

Al Ries & Jack Trout (1981): positioning isn't what you do with the product — it's what you do in the customer's mind. Volvo = safety. Apple = creativity. Nike = performance. Coca-Cola = happiness. Patagonia = environmentalism.

Mind-share precedes market-share. A brand with clear mental positioning sells more with less media, lasts longer, has premium pricing, weathers crises.

### Principle 3 — Differentiation has to be real, demonstrable, defensible

3 tests of real differentiator (Marty Neumeier):
1. **Real** — the brand actually does this (not promise)
2. **Demonstrable** — it can be shown with current product/service
3. **Defensible** — a competitor doesn't copy it in 30 days

"Humanized service" rarely passes the 3 tests — everyone claims it. "Free shipping over R$199" passes: real (clear rule), demonstrable (every purchase), defensible until a competitor matches.

### Principle 4 — Purpose beats price (in mature market)

Jim Stengel (*Grow*): of the 50 global brands with the highest growth from 2000 to 2010, all had articulated purpose beyond profit. Patagonia, Whole Foods, Method, Innocent Drinks, Ben & Jerry's.

But beware: purpose **invented to look like marketing** breaks (Pepsi 2017 with Kendall Jenner). **Foundational** purpose sustains. The difference lies in historical proof + real commercial sacrifice.

Patagonia refused IPO for 30 years because it conflicts with mission. That kind of proof is what sustains purpose.

### Principle 5 — Challenger brand attacks category, not competitor

Geoffrey Moore + Adam Morgan (*Eating the Big Fish*): small brand that attacks big brand by name loses. Small brand that attacks **the entire category** wins.

- Tesla didn't attack GM. It attacked "combustion car".
- Apple iPhone didn't attack Nokia. It attacked "cell phone as tool".
- Liquid Death didn't attack Aquafina. It attacked "boring plastic bottled water".
- Nubank didn't attack Itaú. It attacked "Brazilian bureaucratic bank".

Attacking the category forces the leader to react defensively — it loses either way. Attacking the competitor directly energizes them.

### Principle 6 — Productive tension is a strategic advantage

A strong brand lives a productive tension — two things that would normally be opposites, but it solves together:

- **Apple** — premium + accessible (iPod $399 when the category was $50, but connected to free iTunes)
- **Patagonia** — serious outdoor + declared activism (incompatible for traditional brand)
- **Aesop** — luxury + accessible at point of sale (street store, no jewelry counter)
- **Trader Joe's** — premium quality + average price
- **Nubank** — disruptive + serious (bank that looks non-bank but is a regulated bank)

A brand without tension is commodity. A brand with tension has angle, generates conversation, attracts obsession.

### Principle 7 — Strategy is not a logo decision

Frequent error in Brazilian rebrand: hire design studio, redesign logo, think you repositioned the brand. Logo is manifestation of strategy, not strategy.

Correct sequence:
1. **Strategy** (purpose, positioning, differentiator) — months
2. **Visual identity** (logo, palette, typography, etc.) — weeks
3. **Manifestation** (pieces, communication) — continuous

Inverting kills the investment.

### Principle 8 — Mental availability + Physical availability (Byron Sharp)

Byron Sharp (Ehrenberg-Bass Institute): brands grow by TWO empirical variables, not by "loyalty":

1. **Mental availability** — how easily the brand comes to mind when the consumer thinks of the category
2. **Physical availability** — how easy it is to find/buy when it comes to mind

Both need to be built constantly. "Build base of loyal fans" is popular myth — Sharp shows empirically that real growth comes from increasing penetration (more occasional buyers), not from deepening usage of existing ones.

Implication: brand focuses on reach + availability, not on "engaging super-fans".

### Principle 9 — Long-term brand-building > short-term activation (Binet & Field)

30-year analysis from IPA (Institute of Practitioners in Advertising): brands that allocate **60% to brand-building + 40% to activation** grow more in the long term than brands that invert.

Brand-building: positioning, awareness, emotional association. Doesn't convert immediately.
Activation: performance, conversion, offer. Converts fast but decays fast.

Average Brazilian brand invests 90% in activation (direct ad to cart) and 10% in brand. Result: grows for 6-12 months, then stalls without brand equity.

### Principle 10 — Positioning is exclusion before inclusion

The right question isn't "who is this brand for?" — it's "who is this brand NOT for?". Whom you refuse defines you more than whom you accept.

Ferrari doesn't talk to whoever wants a family SUV. MUJI doesn't talk to whoever wants maximum design statement. Aesop doesn't talk to whoever wants neighborhood pharmacy. Anti-persona is strategy, not moral exclusion.

### Principle 11 — Brand is system, not piece

Brand isn't logo. Brand isn't slogan. Brand isn't campaign. Brand is the **integrated system of all consistent decisions** over years. A brilliant isolated ad doesn't build brand. A thousand consistent decisions do.

Aaker: brand equity is built by **accumulation of coherent associations** over time. Each piece reinforces or weakens. There is no neutral decision.

### Principle 12 — Rebrand is last resort, not first

Rebrand is expensive (financial, mental, accumulated equity). A consolidated brand should prefer **renewal** to redesign. Renewal respects accumulated heritage; rebrand zeros it.

Valid rebrand cases:
- Foundational product change (Apple stopped being only computers)
- Severe reputational crisis (BP post-Deepwater Horizon)
- Merger / acquisition
- Extreme visual lag (>15 years without touch)

Invalid cases:
- "We're tired of the logo"
- "Competitor did a new one, let's do it too"
- "New CMO wants to mark territory"

A brand that rebrands every 5 years never accumulates equity.

## 3. Consolidated strategic frameworks

### 3.1 — Positioning Statement (Geoffrey Moore, *Crossing the Chasm*)

The classic positioning formula. Every brand should be able to write its own:

```
For [specific audience]
who [specific unmet need],
the [brand]
is [market category]
that [unique differentiator],
unlike [main alternative]
because [defensible reason to exist].
```

Example (reference):
> "For Brazilian creators of educational content who need to produce consistently without becoming their own agency, [brand] is a creative operating system that delivers pluggable creative DNA to any AI tool, unlike traditional marketing courses because it sells the ready-made infrastructure, not the learning."

Quality criteria:
- **Audience** specific (not "everyone")
- **Need** real (not brand promise)
- **Category** defined or created
- **Differentiator** demonstrable (Principle 3)
- **Alternative** real (not straw man)
- **Reason to exist** defensible

### 3.2 — Brand Onion (David Aaker)

A brand has concentric layers. From core outward:

1. **Essence** (1-2 words) — soul of the brand
2. **Promise** (1 sentence) — what it delivers
3. **Personality** (3-5 traits) — what it's like as a person
4. **Rational attributes** (features) — what it has
5. **Emotional attributes** (benefits) — how it makes you feel
6. **Visual symbols** (logo, palette, icons) — manifestation

Each layer derives from the previous. Inconsistency between layers = weak brand.

### 3.3 — Brand Identity Prism (Jean-Noël Kapferer)

French model, complementary to Aaker. 6 facets:

1. **Physique** — physical/visual characteristics
2. **Personality** — human character
3. **Culture** — system of values
4. **Relationship** — type of bond with customer
5. **Reflection** — who the customer wants to be perceived as (when using the brand)
6. **Self-image** — who the customer feels they become when using

### 3.4 — BRAND-SCRIPT (Donald Miller, *Building a StoryBrand*)

7-element framework for structuring a message:

1. **Character** — who the customer is (not the brand)
2. **Problem** — the problem they have (3 levels: external, internal, philosophical)
3. **Guide** — the brand as guide (not hero)
4. **Plan** — clear steps to solve
5. **Call to action** — directive, not invitation
6. **Failure** — what happens if they don't act
7. **Success** — transformation if they act

Useful in sales page, ad, institutional pitch.

### 3.5 — Five Forces (Michael Porter)

Classic competitive analysis. Evaluates industry attractiveness by:

1. **Rivalry** among existing competitors
2. **Threat of new entrants**
3. **Power of suppliers**
4. **Power of buyers**
5. **Threat of substitutes**

Useful for understanding where the brand competes and where it has vulnerability.

### 3.6 — Blue Ocean Strategy (Kim & Mauborgne)

Instead of competing in an existing market (red ocean), creating a new market (blue ocean). "Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create" framework:

- **Eliminate** — factors the industry assumes necessary but you cut
- **Reduce** — what you reduce below industry standard
- **Raise** — what you raise above standard
- **Create** — what you offer that no one offers

Cirque du Soleil example: eliminated animals and stars; reduced slapstick humor; raised artistic production; created theatrical narrative.

### 3.7 — Golden Circle (Simon Sinek, *Start with Why*)

3 concentric circles:
1. **Why** (core) — the purpose, why it exists
2. **How** — how it does what it does
3. **What** — what it does

Mediocre brands communicate from outside in (What → How → Why). Memorable brands communicate from inside out (Why → How → What). Apple is the canonical example.

Important criticism: the framework is simplistic (Sinek is a speaker, not a researcher), but useful as an initial articulation tool.

### 3.8 — Jobs-to-be-Done (Clayton Christensen)

Customer doesn't buy product — hires product to do a "job":
- Functional job (solve practical problem)
- Emotional job (feel a specific way)
- Social job (be perceived by others in a specific way)

Analysis: "when [context], I want [functional], so I can [emotional/social]".

Example (Christensen on McDonald's milkshake):
> "When I'm in morning traffic with 30min of driving to work, I want a liquid snack that lasts the entire trip and distracts me, so I can arrive at work full and without having made a mess in the car."

JTBD replaces "demography" as base of segmentation.

### 3.9 — Ehrenberg-Bass / How Brands Grow (Byron Sharp)

Empirical principles based on decades of data:

1. **Penetration** (more buyers) > Loyalty (existing buyers buying more)
2. **Mental availability** + **Physical availability** = growth
3. **Distinctive Brand Assets** (consistent colors, sounds, symbols) > differentiation messaging
4. **Light buyers** matter more than heavy buyers
5. **Mass marketing** works; "narrowcasting" rarely
6. **Brand growth** comes from doubling penetration, not doubling usage

Criticism: mainly applicable to volume categories (FMCG). Debatable in niche B2B. But widely empirically validated.

## 4. The 6 strategic pillars of DNA

Every brand DNA articulates 6 strategic elements. Without articulating them, there is no strategy — there is operation.

### 4.1 — Purpose (Why)

**Definition:** the foundational reason for existing. If the brand closed today, what stops existing in the world?

**Quality criterion:** the sentence has a concrete noun that stops existing. "Inspire people" is not concrete. "The possibility of [audience] [doing specific X] without [concrete friction]" is concrete.

**Canonical examples:**
- Patagonia: "We're in business to save our home planet."
- Nike: "To bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world."
- Tesla: "To accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy."

### 4.2 — Mission (What we do)

**Definition:** what we do today, operationally, to fulfill the purpose.

**Quality criterion:** verbs in the present indicative (we build, we sell, we teach), not aspiration (we want, we dream).

### 4.3 — Vision (Where we're going)

**Definition:** the end-state we pursue in 5-10 years.

**Quality criterion:** can you draw the vision? A concrete vision can be drawn as a scene. An abstract vision ("be a global reference") cannot.

**Examples:**
- Microsoft (Bill Gates, 1980s): "A computer on every desk and in every home."
- SpaceX: "Make humanity multiplanetary."

### 4.4 — Positioning (Where we play, How we win)

Geoffrey Moore format (section 3.1). Documented in full in DNA.md.

### 4.5 — Central promise (Pact with audience)

**Definition:** if the audience uses the product, what is the functional + emotional result?

**Format:** "If [audience] [uses brand], then [functional result] + [emotional result]."

**Criterion:** it has two components — functional (what they can DO) + emotional (how they FEEL / are PERCEIVED). Without emotional component, it's just feature.

### 4.6 — Key insight (The "ah-ha")

**Definition:** the counterintuitive observation that justifies the product existing.

**Criterion:** the sentence has **a negation** OR **an inversion** OR **a productive tension**. If it's just a positive statement, it's not insight — it's decoration.

**Examples:**
- "Brand identity is not a PDF file no one reads — it's the set of rules every AI needs to read before producing." (inversion)
- "People don't buy drills. They buy holes in the wall." (Levitt — perspective inversion)
- "The most sustainable outdoor gear is the one you don't buy." (Patagonia — commercial premise inversion)

## 5. Competitive map — how to do it

### 5.1 — Identification of the 3 closest competitors

Criterion: who the customer considers in the same choice set. They are not all in the sector — they are the ones who dispute the real decision.

For each competitor, map:
- Current perceived positioning
- Identifiable strengths
- Visible fragilities
- What you do differently (Principle 3 — real, demonstrable, defensible)

### 5.2 — White space — what NO ONE does

The most valuable analysis. Real gaps in the market that no one covers. Usually where the best differentiator lives.

Historical example: Tesla saw that no one made an aspirational premium electric car (Prius was a utilitarian hybrid hatch). It occupied white space.

Brazilian example: Nubank saw that no one made a bank with good UX + no fees + human language for Brazilian millennials. It occupied white space.

### 5.3 — Category we want to redefine

A strong brand doesn't compete in the existing category — it defines a new category or redefines an existing one.

Difference:
- **Current category** — where you compete today, perceived by the market
- **Category you want to create** — how you want to be perceived in 3-5 years

When both are equal, the brand is commodity. When different, the brand has angle.

## 6. Real vs. perceived differentiation

### 6.1 — Types of differentiator

Aaker classifies:

1. **Functional** — product does something better (faster, more durable, cheaper)
2. **Emotional** — feeling when using (Coke = happiness, Volvo = safety)
3. **Self-expressive** — user expresses identity when using (Apple = creative, Patagonia = conscious)
4. **Social** — connection to tribe (Harley = motorcyclist, CrossFit = community)

A strong brand combines 2-3 types. A weak brand has only functional (commodity).

### 6.2 — Real vs. perceived differentiation

- **Real differentiation** — product/service actually superior in some dimension
- **Perceived differentiation** — customer believes it's superior (even if it's not)

Surprise: perceived is often more valuable than real (Pepsi usually beats Coke in blind taste tests, but Coke sells more — because brand > product in decision).

Most robust strategy: combines real (can't be false) + perceived (needs to communicate).

### 6.3 — Defensibility

An undefended differentiator is commodity in 12-24 months. Defensibility comes from:

- **Network effects** (more users = more value)
- **Brand equity** (accumulated over decades)
- High **switching cost** for customer
- **Patents or IP**
- **Economies of scale** (only big player gets the price)
- **Privileged location/distribution**
- **Internal culture** hard to replicate
- **Deep supplier relationships**

A small brand usually has as defensibility: brand equity + culture. That's why building brand matters.

## 7. Negative scope — what NOT to do

The most revealing question for strategy: "what do you NOT do, deliberately, even though you could?"

Brands that answer "we do everything the customer asks" have no strategy. They have operation.

Brands with clear negative scope (examples):
- **Apple** — doesn't make cheap product (strategic refusal of massive low-end market)
- **Patagonia** — doesn't sell in traditional Black Friday, doesn't sell to Amazon (channel refusal)
- **In-N-Out Burger** — doesn't expand to a new state if it can't guarantee meat quality (growth refusal)
- **Aesop** — doesn't do lifestyle product line (extension refusal)

Declared negative scope:
- Defines focus (what's left is priority)
- Refuses briefs quickly (doesn't fit the scope)
- Attracts aligned talent (whoever accepts restriction endures culture)
- Sustains positioning (consistency)

A brand without negative scope dilutes in 5 years.

## 8. GOOD vs BAD strategy (canonical cases)

### 8.1 — GOOD strategy (to study)

**Iconic global cases:**

- **Apple (1997-2024)** — Steve Jobs returned, reduced portfolio from 350 products to 4, defined "creative tools for creative people" positioning, invested in design and brand-building, refused licensing. 27 years of consistent execution.
- **Patagonia (1973-present)** — Yvon Chouinard founded it in 1973 with environmental mission. "Don't Buy This Jacket" (NYT, 2011). 1% for the planet since 1985. Donated company to environmental foundation in 2022.
- **Nike** — "Just Do It" (1988) + Air Jordan + Colin Kaepernick. Sustained brand-building.
- **Tesla** — attacked category (combustion), created premium aspiration for electric, brand without significant ad spend (earned media through execution).
- **Liquid Death** — attacked "boring plastic bottled water", positioned itself as heavy metal lifestyle. Grew from zero to $700M in 5 years without traditional media.

**Brazilian cases:**

- **Nubank (2013+)** — attacked "Brazilian bureaucratic bank", human language ("disposition to be loved"), distinctive purple card. Largest LATAM fintech IPO.
- **Magazine Luiza (2015+)** — Lu as AI mascot, mass human communication, sustained digital transformation. From regional retailer to dominant national player.
- **Granado** — 1870 heritage, careful modernization without losing Brazilian apothecary DNA.
- **Havaianas** — 90s relaunch under Marcio Kogan + Almap BBDO. From poor person's flip-flop to global fashion icon.
- **Açaí Brazil** — Brazilian açaí brand developed for export, premium positioning.

### 8.2 — BAD strategy (to avoid)

**Saturated anti-patterns:**

- **"Being everything to everyone"** — dilution that pushes away niche without gaining mass
- **"Pivoting every 18 months"** — without giving any positioning time to build equity
- **"Copying the leader"** — Pepsi, Hyundai, Asus copying Coke, Toyota, Apple — always perpetual 2nd place
- **"Performative brand purpose"** — using social cause without historical proof (Pepsi 2017, Bud Light 2023)
- **"Confusing marketing with product"** — brilliant campaigns for mediocre product — frustration scales
- **"Investing 100% in activation"** — short-term sales, zero brand equity, no pricing power
- **"Logo redesign every 3 years"** — never accumulates recognition

**Brazilian cases to study as how NOT to do** (anonymous):
- Personal coach brands that change positioning at every launch
- Traditional Brazilian banks that try to be "modern fintech" without changing internal bureaucracy (reads false)
- Companies that adopt "environmental purpose" without changing real operation (greenwashing)

## 9. Strategy that WORKS vs DOESN'T WORK (at scale)

### 9.1 — WORKS

- **Focused positioning** sustained for 10+ years
- **Investment in brand-building** (60% of budget, minimum)
- **Distinctive brand assets** consistent (color, symbol, jingle, voice)
- **Declared negative scope** (refusal of outside opportunities)
- **Real purpose with historical proof** (not invented for marketing)
- **Mental availability** wide (top of mind in the category)
- **Physical availability** wide (easy to find/buy)
- **Executional consistency** across all touchpoints
- **Visionary leadership** with time (CEO/founder present)
- **Internal culture aligned** with brand promise

### 9.2 — DOESN'T WORK

- **Positioning that changes every 2 years**
- **Investment 100% in performance** (no brand-building)
- **Logo/identity constantly redesigned** (zeros equity)
- **Trying to serve everyone** (commodity)
- **Invented brand purpose** for marketing (burns when discovered)
- **Inconsistent communication** across channels (acidic caption + institutional email)
- **Generic differentiator** ("quality", "service")
- **Focus only on super-fans** (Sharp shows it doesn't scale)
- **CEO absent from brand** (high marketing turnover)
- **Misaligned internal culture** (customer feels friction)

## 10. Strategy that SELLS vs REPELS

### 10.1 — SELLS

- **Specific and deliverable promise** (concrete > abstract)
- **Social proof with real names** (B2B: customer logos; B2C: identifiable testimonials)
- **Clear category** (customer knows what they're buying)
- **Articulated defensible differentiator** (1 memorable sentence)
- **Audience's language** (not internal jargon)
- **Unequivocal CTA** (clear next step)
- **Guarantee/risk reversal** when relevant (return, trial)

### 10.2 — REPELS

- **Exaggerated promise** ("will change your life" — zero credibility)
- **Confusing positioning** (customer doesn't know what it's for)
- **"Unique in the market"** (usually false, discrediting)
- **Direct comparison with named competitor** (becomes fight, loses on both sides)
- **Soulless institutional discourse** ("our mission is to delight customers")
- **Marketing that promises X, product delivers Y** (burns reputation)
- **Strategic pivot published on LinkedIn** ("we changed direction!") — generates distrust

## 11. Strategy that AGES WELL vs DATES FAST

### 11.1 — AGES WELL

- **Purpose anchored in universal human value** (health, connection, creativity, safety)
- **Positioning in lasting category** (not in tech trend)
- **Structural differentiator** (not dependent on specific tech)
- **Human archetypal personality** (caring, rebel, sage, magician — Jung)
- **Classic visual symbols** (Nike swoosh, Apple maçã, Mercedes star)

### 11.2 — DATES FAST

- **Positioning in "trend of the moment"** (NFT, crypto, metaverse, AI — without deeper anchoring)
- **Visual following "what's trending"** (purple gradient 2020-2022, glassmorphism)
- **Generation-specific language** that ages in 3 years ("OK boomer", "no cap", "rizz")
- **Promise tied to specific tech** ("powered by GPT-3" — dated in 6 months)
- **CEO as mandatory public persona** (vulnerable to personal scandal)

### 11.3 — Principle of strategic longevity

> "Brands aren't made to last; brands are remade to last."
>
> — Marty Neumeier

A brand that lasts 50 years was carefully repositioned at the right moments, without losing essence. It wasn't rebrand — it was evolution. Coca-Cola had 30+ campaigns, but "happiness" as gut feeling remained.

## 12. Strategic anti-patterns

- ❌ Positioning as "market leader" without being so
- ❌ "Unique in the world" without proof
- ❌ Performative brand purpose (without aligned operation)
- ❌ Trying to serve 3+ distinct personas with the same communication
- ❌ Generic differentiator ("quality", "service", "innovation")
- ❌ Pivoting positioning at every launch
- ❌ Copying leader's positioning
- ❌ Ignoring negative scope
- ❌ Investing 100% in activation, 0% in brand
- ❌ Confusing "brand" with "logo"
- ❌ Believing rebrand solves strategic problem
- ❌ Strategic decisions made by committee (becomes soulless average)
- ❌ Aspirational positioning without real execution ("we want to be a reference")

## 13. Quality criteria

### 13.1 — The 7 criteria

1. **Articulable in 1 sentence** — without clear articulation, it's dispersion
2. **Specific** — fits this brand, not any brand in the category
3. **Defensible** — competitor doesn't copy it in 30 days
4. **Demonstrable** — current product/service proves it
5. **Coherent** — aligned with voice, visual, behavior of DNA
6. **Operational** — guides real day-to-day decisions
7. **Sustainable** — valid for 10+ years

5+ "yes" = competent strategy. 7/7 = excellent strategy.

### 13.2 — The refusal test

A brand with clear strategy refuses generic briefs quickly ("doesn't fit our positioning"). A brand without strategy debates every opportunity.

Question: in the last 6 months, how many opportunities have you explicitly refused? If zero, strategy is missing.

### 13.3 — The new employee test

A new employee should understand the brand's positioning in 1 hour of reading. If it takes weeks, strategy is poorly documented (or is vague).
