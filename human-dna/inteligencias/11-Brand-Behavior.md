## 0. Doctrine

A brand isn't what it says it is. It's what it does when no one asked. Behavior — in DM, comment, transactional email, response to controversy, gratitude, refusal — defines the brand as much as visual identity and tone of voice. Often more.

The biggest difference between aspirational brand and admired brand is operational behavior. Aspirational brand speaks beautifully in campaign, but mistreats in DM. Admired brand behaves consistently across every touchpoint, even when no one is paying attention. Aesop responds to DMs with the same care it writes catalog. Patagonia behaves with the same ethics in customer service as in public campaign. Apple returns a product with the same packaging standard as a new product.

The biggest source of brand erosion in Brazil is the disconnect between communication and behavior. Brand promised "humanized service" and responds DM with a bot. Brand promised "transparency" and hides return policy. Brand promised "brand purpose" and fires 10% silently. The customer perceives the disconnect — and the perception erodes the brand more than any external criticism.

Every decision in this document starts from a premise: **brand behavior is strategy executed at micro scale**. Every DM, every email, every response to criticism — is a moment of proof. Brand consistent at micro scale beats brand brilliant in occasional campaign.

## 2. The 12 founding principles

### Principle 1 — Behavior is more audited than communication

Customer reads 1 caption per month. But interacts with the brand 10+ times (DMs, emails, comments). Operational behavior accumulates 10x more touchpoints than institutional communication. Brand that takes care only of the brilliant (campaign) and neglects the operational (DM, email) has public narrative vs lived reality.

### Principle 2 — Every interaction is editorial

Response to a negative comment has the same care as a carousel post. Transactional email follows the same editorial principle as marketing email. DM is brand piece, not private conversation — DM screenshot goes viral with the same ease as a post.

Implication: support team needs to understand brand voice as well as the copy team.

### Principle 3 — Honesty > politeness

A brand that says "maybe in the future" cordially to a refusal it knows is definitive burns reputation when discovered. A brand that says "from what you described, I don't think we're the best partner for this moment" honestly preserves reputation AND qualifies future prospects.

Direct honesty with respectful tone beats evasive politeness. Always.

### Principle 4 — Speed > perfect precision in crisis

In crisis, the first hour defines the narrative. Imperfect quick response > perfect response 12h later. Prolonged silence becomes a story about silence (Streisand effect).

Coombs (SCCT): standard crisis comms protocol is to respond in 1h, even if partial. Progressive update.

### Principle 5 — Assume before explaining

In crisis: "We were wrong. [Fact.] We already corrected. Next step: [concrete action]." Not: "We regret any inconvenience that may have occurred. We are investigating."

Assuming builds trust. Explaining before assuming looks defensive.

### Principle 6 — No blaming third party

"It was a supplier failure", "It was a system problem", "It was an isolated employee action" — all erode reputation. The brand assumes responsibility for what it operates. Period.

Emblematic case: Tylenol 1982 (product poisoning by third party). J&J took responsibility, total recall, absolute transparency. Today canonical crisis management case.

### Principle 7 — Concrete action with deadline > vague promise

Bad crisis: "We are investigating."
Good crisis: "We identified the cause at [X]. We fixed today. Refund for everyone affected in 5 business days. Weekly update here on the profile until completely resolved."

Action + deadline. Always.

### Principle 8 — Documented SLA is commitment

A brand that promises "we respond in 24h" and doesn't fulfill burns more than a brand that says "we respond in 3 business days" and fulfills. SLA is an implicit contract.

Recommended standard per channel:
- Instagram DM (business hours): 4h
- Transactional email: immediate (system)
- Human email: 24 business hours
- Public comment: 24 business hours
- Crisis level 3+: 1-6h

### Principle 9 — Tone modulates by channel, voice never

Voice (brand personality) is fixed across every touchpoint. Tone modulates:

- Intimate DM: "Get this, that's probably..."
- Transactional email: "Payment confirmed. Next step: [link]."
- Public crisis: "We were wrong. [Fact.] Fixed."

Same brand, distinct contexts. Customer who knows the brand through DM recognizes the voice in crisis comms. Coherence sustains trust.

### Principle 10 — Behavioral calendar is strategy

Brands have dates they **always touch** (annual launch, anniversary) and dates they **don't touch** (politicized, saturated, out of scope). Strategic decision, not improvisation.

A brand that touches generic Mother's Day without relation to the theme looks opportunistic. A brand that's silent on an expected date (Black Friday if it's an anti-consumption brand) looks coherent.

### Principle 11 — Emoji policy is decision, not accident

Emoji is tone decision. Formal brand doesn't use fire 🔥 in DM. Irreverent brand does. Conscious decision, documented.

Recurring anti-pattern: brand that oscillates between formal (no emoji) and casual (10 emojis) without criteria, depending on who responds. Sign of lack of documented brand behavior.

### Principle 12 — Internal culture aligns (or disconnects) brand promise

Brand promises "human service" but the internal team is treated with bureaucratic pressure. Brand promises "environmental" but the office consumes disposable plastic. Brand promises "diversity" but leadership is homogeneous.

Customer perceives the disconnect. Usually takes 2-5 years. When they perceive it, it burns fast. Internal culture is the foundation of brand behavior.

Patagonia positive example: internal environmentalist culture (they pay employees to do environmental volunteer work, headquarters with solar energy, fired to achieve net zero) sustains brand promise.

## 3. Consolidated frameworks (with attribution)

### 3.1 — Customer Experience Maturity (Forrester)

5 stages of CX maturity:

1. **Reactive** — puts out fires
2. **Defined** — standardized process
3. **Managed** — metrics + dashboards
4. **Driving** — CX guides product decision
5. **Embedded** — CX is culture, not department

A small brand is usually in Reactive or Defined. Maturity takes 5-10 years.

### 3.2 — SCCT — Situational Crisis Communication Theory (Timothy Coombs)

3 types of crisis:
1. **Victim** — the company is the victim (natural disaster, sabotage, false rumor)
2. **Accident** — accident without intent (product defect, employee error)
3. **Preventable** — the company clearly erred (negligence, bad faith, illegality)

Each type requires a distinct response:
- Victim: justified victimization, focus on recovery
- Accident: explanation + corrective action
- Preventable: explicit apology + substantial reparation + structural change

Wrong response for the wrong type makes the situation worse.

### 3.3 — STEPPS virality framework (Jonah Berger)

6 factors that make content viral:

1. **Social currency** — sharing makes you look intelligent/interesting
2. **Triggers** — something in the environment reminds of the product
3. **Emotion** — strong emotion (positive or negative)
4. **Public** — public visibility of use
5. **Practical value** — real utility
6. **Stories** — embedded narrative

Applied to brand behavior: how the brand builds moments customers want to share.

### 3.4 — Hug Your Haters (Jay Baer)

Service to angry customer on public social media:

- Respond publicly FIRST (whoever saw the criticism sees the response)
- Move to private channel to resolve detail
- Come back public only when resolved (to show resolution)
- Never delete criticism (Streisand effect)
- Never block critic (becomes post about blocking)

### 3.5 — Service Design Blueprint

Visual map of customer journey in 3 layers:
1. **Frontstage** — what the customer sees (touchpoints)
2. **Backstage** — what the team sees (systems, processes)
3. **Underground** — infrastructure (tech, legal, ops)

Identifies gaps where frontstage promises what backstage doesn't deliver.

### 3.6 — RACE (Reach, Act, Convert, Engage)

Customer behavior framework in funnel:
1. **Reach** — attract attention
2. **Act** — first interaction
3. **Convert** — conversion (purchase)
4. **Engage** — continuous relationship

Brand behavior optimizes each stage.

- Secure payment button if transacting

Document protocol according to the brand's real presence. Don't create presence on a channel without defining behavior first.

## 5. Crisis playbook — 5 levels

### Level 1 — Confusion

**Symptom:** individual comment about doubt or dissatisfaction.

**Response:**
- Response on the channel same day
- Standard brand voice
- Validation + clarification

**Example:**
> Customer: "I didn't understand if shipping is free for all states."
> Brand: "Free shipping above R$199 to all of Brazil. Below that, we calculate by ZIP code. Any specific question, send your ZIP in DM and I'll confirm."

### Level 2 — Alert

**Symptom:** several comments about the same issue, within 24h.

**Response:**
- Response on the channel + DMs to the vocal ones
- Consider pinned post clarifying
- Document internally for structural solution

**Example:**
> 5+ comments complaining about shipping delay.
> Brand: pinned comment: "We had a delay in shipping this week due to [concrete reason]. Already normalized. Whoever was affected, send DM with order to follow up."

### Level 3 — Small crisis

**Symptom:** issue gained local visibility (viral post in comment, screenshot circulating, story from creator citing).

**Response:**
- Formal institutional response within 6h
- Own post explaining situation + action
- Proactive DMs to those who were affected

**Communiqué template:**
```
[Today], [event that happened / wrong decision].

Context: [honest explanation, in 2-3 sentences].

What we're doing:
• [Concrete action 1] — [deadline]
• [Concrete action 2] — [deadline]

We feel [genuine emotion without hyperbolizing]. For those directly affected, [concrete reparation offer].

If you have questions, talk to us: [direct channel].

— [Name of person who signs, usually founder]
```

### Level 4 — Big crisis

**Symptom:** issue became news (article, post from big creator, Twitter thread with 1k+ likes).

**Response:**
- Institutional response within 2h
- Formal communiqué across all channels
- May include video story from founder
- Eventual withdrawal of problematic product/post
- Proactive communication with press

### Level 5 — Systemic crisis

**Symptom:** issue threatens reputation long term (legal, ethical, formal complaint).

**Response:**
- Legal response + coordinated communication
- Total transparency
- Eventually, structural change (firing, product change)
- Active follow-up for 6-12 months

### Universal principles of crisis comms

1. **Speed > perfect precision** — first response in defined window
2. **Assume BEFORE explaining** — "We were wrong." opens, then comes context
3. **No blaming third party** — supplier, partner, system, AI — internal blame always
4. **No passive-aggressive** — no "I'm sorry you felt that way"
5. **Concrete action with deadline** — every response has action + date
6. **Single channel authorized to speak** — one designated person during crisis
7. **Document everything** — timeline, decisions, result for learning

### Post-crisis

- Document timeline + decisions + result
- Add to crisis history (to learn)
- Update process if something in the system allowed the error
- Evaluate at 30/60/90 days the reputational impact

## 6. Behavioral calendar

### 6.1 — Dates we always touch

Define 3-5 mandatory dates per year. Criterion: has direct relation with brand/category.

Examples:
- **Brand anniversary** — celebratory + reflective tone, without hyperbolizing
- **Annual launch** — full campaign
- **Black Friday** — defined promo, without fabricated urgency
- **End of year** — editorial reflection, public balance
- **Specific niche dates** (e.g.: coffee brand marks International Coffee Day)

### 6.2 — Dates we DON'T touch

Strategic decision to silence deliberately. Reasons:

- **Politicized dates** (election, polarizations) — brand doesn't take partisan position
- **Saturated dates** (generic Mother's Day) — touching becomes forced
- **Out-of-scope dates** (Yellow September if the brand has no real cause there)
- **Dates of collective mourning** — respectful silence, not publicity

### 6.3 — Dates we touch carefully / only if relevant

Gray cases where decision depends on context:
- Broad commemorative dates (Christmas) — touch if there's authentic proposal
- Founder / team birthdays (internal dates) — touch if it fits in the public brand

### 6.4 — Principle of coherent silence

A brand that silences at expected moment **communicates position without words**. Patagonia ignores traditional Black Friday (and sometimes does "Don't Buy This Jacket"). Aesop doesn't do aggressive Mother's Day push. Coherent silence is a declaration.

## 7. Onboarding and offboarding

### 7.1 — Onboarding (new customer)

Canonical 7-day sequence after first purchase:

| Day | Touchpoint | Content |
|---|---|---|
| 0 (purchase) | Transactional email | Confirmation, next step, access link |
| 0 (purchase) | DM | Founder welcome, in personal text (not copy-paste) |
| 1 | Email | 7-minute quickstart — link, short video |
| 3 | Email | Use case #1 — concrete example from week 1 |
| 7 | Email | Check-in — "how's it going?" honest, offers question channel |
| 14 | DM | Personal check — founder checks if everything's OK |
| 30 | Email | Survey NPS + invitation to community |

Adapt according to product type. Principles:
- Progressive spacing (not forced daily)
- Mix touchpoints (email + DM)
- Real question, not performative engagement
- Next step always concrete

### 7.2 — Offboarding (cancellation, end of contract)

Cancellation is a reputation moment. Policy:

- **Self-service cancellation?** Yes, with 1 click. No friction.
- **Cancellation survey?** 1 optional question ("what would change for you to come back?"). No "are you sure?" button
- **Goodbye email?** Yes — thanks, reinforces data is safe, keeps door open.
- **Post-cancellation re-contact?** Not in 90 days. After that, generic newsletter OK if the person didn't withdraw consent.
- **Reactivation?** Customer who comes back DOESN'T get extra discount (insults whoever stayed). Gets warm welcome and personal check-in.

## 9. GOOD vs BAD behavior (canonical cases)

### 9.1 — GOOD behavior (study)

**Global reference brands:**

- **Zappos** — service as strategic differentiator. No script, no timer. Founder Tony Hsieh wrote *Delivering Happiness*.
- **JetBlue** — post-crisis "Valentine's Day Massacre" 2007: CEO published apology video within 24h, created "Customer Bill of Rights", active refund. Reputation recovered.
- **Patagonia** — coherent activism: donated company to environmental foundation 2022. Brand behavior aligned with brand promise.
- **Domino's** — "Pizza Turnaround" 2010: responded self-critically to videos of employees ruining orders. Reformulated product publicly. Recovered reputation.
- **Nubank** — "willingness to be loved" as philosophy. Human service at scale, direct communication without jargon.
- **Magazine Luiza** — Lu as AI mascot + human communication at scale. Digital transformation sustained by consistent behavior.

**Characteristics in common:**
- Response speed in crisis
- Honesty about error
- Concrete action with deadline
- Consistent voice across every touchpoint
- Internal culture aligned with brand promise

### 9.2 — BAD behavior (avoid)

**Canonical anti-cases:**

- **United Airlines 2017** — "Dragged passenger". CEO initially defended employees ("re-accommodate" passenger). Response was worse than the act itself. Shares fell 4% that day. Case became reference of "how NOT to do crisis comms".
- **BP Deepwater Horizon 2010** — CEO Tony Hayward said "I'd like my life back" while spill devastated coast. Fired in months.
- **Pepsi Kendall Jenner 2017** — performative brand purpose ad (Kendall ends protest delivering Pepsi to police). Withdrawn in 24h. Case of invented purpose.
- **Bud Light 2023** — collaboration with Dylan Mulvaney + contradictory behavior afterwards (now support, now distance). Massive boycott, structural sales drop.

**Saturated anti-patterns:**
- "Investigating" response without deadline
- Blocking/deleting criticism
- Mass positive comment (bot) discovered
- Standard "thanks for following" auto-DM spam
- Proactively asking for review/like in DM

## 10. Behavior that SELLS vs REPELS

### 10.1 — SELLS

- **Quick response** — DM 4h, comment 24h
- **Human service** — real person responds, not bot in real conversation
- **Careful onboarding** — 7-30 day sequence with care
- **Brand tone in customer service** — coherence between brilliant and operational
- **Founder present** — on social network, in emails, in moment of crisis
- **Resolution, not just response** — problem solved instead of "we'll check"
- **Authentic reciprocity** — real gratitude when customer does something good

### 10.2 — REPELS

- **Bot in real conversation** (customer perceives and loses trust)
- **Generic automatic response** ("we appreciate your contact")
- **Unfulfilled SLA** (promises 24h, responds in 5 days)
- **Inconsistent voice** between channels (acidic caption + institutional email)
- **Silenced crisis** or handled with defensiveness
- **Intrusive mass marketing** (DM spam, unsolicited email)
- **Asking for 5-star review in exchange for something** (anti-practice)
- **Blocking critic** (becomes news)

### 10.3 — Commercial principle

Excellent operational behavior costs dearly (person, time, training) — but it's the barrier that protects margin. Brand with brand promise aligned with real behavior charges premium and has high retention. Disconnected brand lives off constant acquisition (expensive, unstable).

## 11. Behavior that AGES WELL vs DATES FAST

### 11.1 — AGES WELL

- **Direct honesty** (timeless — Frank Sonnenberg)
- **Speed in crisis** (timeless)
- **Human service at scale** (harder today, more valuable)
- **Clear documented policy** (rarely changes)
- **Voice consistent across decades** (Aesop, Apple)

### 11.2 — DATES FAST

- **Brand tone trends** (Wendy's 2017 sarcasm became dated by 2022)
- **Generation-specific catchphrases** ("OK boomer", "no cap" — age out in 3 years)
- **Public controversy as tactic** (depletes narrative in months)
- **"Challenger" positioning as permanent** (becomes incumbent eventually)

## 12. Behavioral anti-patterns

### 12.1 — On channel

- ❌ Deleting critical comments (non-spam) — Streisand effect
- ❌ Blocking complainers — becomes post about blocking
- ❌ Mass positive comment (bot) — always discovered
- ❌ Auto-DM "thanks for following" — standard spam
- ❌ Proactively asking for review/like in DM — spam behavior
- ❌ Reposting customer story without permission — image rights
- ❌ Sharing DM screenshot without consent — ethical
- ❌ Over-"personalizing" (calling by name 4x in the same message) — false intimacy

### 12.2 — In crisis

- ❌ Prolonged silence (>6h in level 3+ crisis)
- ❌ Communiqué written by lawyer without brand review
- ❌ Blaming third party (supplier, AI, "system")
- ❌ Joking about the crisis
- ❌ Pretending it was nothing ("a small misunderstanding")
- ❌ Posting content "as if nothing happened" before communicating
- ❌ Generic apology without concrete action

### 12.3 — In commercial conflict

- ❌ Accepting out-of-scope project "because the customer insisted"
- ❌ Giving discount without strategic justification
- ❌ Extending deadline "to accommodate" when it means cutting quality
- ❌ Accepting late payment without interest recurrently
- ❌ Speaking ill of competitor publicly

## 13. Quality criteria

### 13.1 — The 7 criteria

1. **SLA met** — response within declared deadline
2. **Consistent voice** — same tone across all channels
3. **Real resolution** — problem solved (not just answered)
4. **Honesty** — without evasion, without blaming third party
5. **Clear next action** — always indicates what's coming
6. **Aligned culture** — brand promise reflected in internal operation
7. **Speed in crisis** — first response in defined window

5+ "yes" = competent behavior. 7/7 = excellent.

### 13.2 — The screenshot test

Take any brand interaction (DM, email, comment) and imagine the screenshot circulating publicly. Would you publish it with pride? If you hesitate, behavior is below standard.

### 13.3 — The reverse test

Imagine your brand serving YOU. Would you be satisfied? Would you buy again? Would you be sincere?

A brand that wouldn't serve its own founder well has a systemic problem.
