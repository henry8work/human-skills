## 0. Doctrine

Photography communicates before the word. The human brain processes image in 13 milliseconds. Before the person reads "serious branding", they've already decided "this brand is serious" or "this brand is amateur" by the quality of the first photo they saw.

The biggest difference between premium brand and average brand in 2026 is photography. Bad typography, palette without refinement, improvised layout — all have a solution in 1 week. **Bad photography takes months to calibrate and usually requires real investment (photographer, lighting, direction, model).** That's why it's where small brands give themselves away most — and where brands that invest more distinguish themselves.

A brand's image isn't "pretty photo". It's **coherent visual direction that communicates positioning without words.** Aesop doesn't sell cream — it sells apothecary ritual. And it sells this through photography. Patagonia doesn't sell jackets — it sells outdoor ethics. And it sells this through photography. Apple doesn't sell phones — it sells precise objects. And it sells this through photography.

Every decision in this document starts from a premise: **brand photography is direction, not capture**. Camera is tool. Director is who decides. Without direction (light, composition, mood, moment, treatment), photo is description. With direction, photo is statement.

## 2. The 12 founding principles

### Principle 1 — Direction is decision, capture is execution

Brand photography isn't "going there to photograph and see what comes out". It's **deciding everything before the click**: light setup, camera position, composition, wardrobe, expression, mood, intended color grade. The click is the last 10% of the work.

Amateur photographer prepares in 5 minutes and photographs for 2 hours. Senior photographer prepares for 2 hours and photographs in 30 minutes. The difference is in the result.

### Principle 2 — Light first, everything else after

Every photo is, at heart, **a problem of light**. Direction of light, quality of light, temperature of light, shadows generated by light. Before thinking about model, about camera, about pose — think about light.

The 7 canonical setups (section 3) cover 95% of needs. Whoever masters the 7 photographs professionally in any condition.

### Principle 3 — Specific beats generic, always

"Pretty photo of person" is empty description. "32-year-old woman, natural loose hair, wearing graphite-gray blazer, in lateral light from north-facing window at 10am, expression of slight concentration looking out of frame, blurred background of old bookshelf" is direction.

The difference between brief that produces banal photo vs. memorable photo is in **specificity of direction**. Each element decided. Nothing left to improvisation.

### Principle 4 — Decisive moment (Cartier-Bresson)

Henri Cartier-Bresson defined the concept that organizes all authorial documentary photography: the **decisive instant** is the fraction of a second in which **geometry + action + emotion** align. Before or after, it's just photo. In that instant, it's photography.

Practical implication: brand photo not posed, always patient. Wait for the natural gesture, the real instant. Artificial posed photo is dead before printing.

### Principle 5 — Selective saturation

The difference between saturated commercial photo (catalog) and sophisticated editorial photo (Apartamento, The Gentlewoman) is in selective saturation: **desaturated background + 1 vivid point of color**.

The eye immediately goes to the saturated point. It becomes the narrative focus. Recurring use: product in the brand's primary color + environment in warm neutrals.

### Principle 6 — Depth of field is narrative

Flat photo (everything in focus) = factual journalism.
Photo with shallow DOF (selective focus) = authorial direction, hierarchy.

Premium brand almost always uses shallow DOF in portraits and product: communicates intentionality. Informative brand uses deep DOF: communicates clarity. Strategic decision, not aesthetic.

### Principle 7 — Crop is decision, not chance

Aspect ratio + crop define the reading. Square (1:1) is balanced, neutral. 4:5 is vertical editorial. 16:9 is cinematic. 9:16 is mobile-first vertical. 21:9 is extreme cinematic.

Crop within the frame (closeup vs wide shot) defines narrative hierarchy: closeup intimacy, wide shot context. Direction thinks before the click.

### Principle 8 — Color is mood before description

Color grading isn't "make it pretty". It's **defining mood**. Wong Kar-wai uses saturated green-blue for urban loneliness. Roger Deakins uses dense amber for nostalgia (1917). Christopher Doyle uses washed color for contained despair.

Brand has a canonical color grade. Every photo passes through it. Without that, brand has 100 photos of 100 places — has no visual identity.

### Principle 9 — Obsessive anti-stock

The most dangerous photography for a brand is stock photography. Not because it's illegal — because it's generic. Stock photo is detectable in 2 seconds by any trained eye. And it signals "this brand doesn't invest in itself".

Rule: never identifiable stock photo. When you need an image you can't produce, prefer (in order):
1. Own authorial photo
2. Photo from contracted Brazilian photographer
3. Carefully curated stock from Death to Stock, Stocksy, Twenty20 (not generic Getty/Shutterstock)
4. AI generation with specific direction (last resort, with care)

### Principle 10 — Real person, not corporate pose

The corporate pose died in 2015. Catalog smile, crossed arms, handshake in the meeting room — all of this reads today as amateur, dated, fake.

Contemporary brand photography captures **real person in real action** or **controlled editorial portrait** (without forced smile). Avedon white background, Lindbergh contemplative black and white, Leibovitz editorial conceptual.

### Principle 11 — Environment tells story

Photo of a person isn't only about the person. It's about the person **in the environment**. Details around (books on the shelf, half-drunk coffee, personal object) carry personality. Neutral background can communicate focus; loaded background can communicate cultural context.

Decision is strategic: how much context enters. Minimalist brand (Aesop) uses very clean background. Cultural brand (Apartamento) uses loaded environment.

### Principle 12 — Video is photography in motion, not another discipline

Photography principles (light, composition, moment, color grade) apply directly to video. Roger Deakins is cinematographer because he thinks first like a photographer. Beginner video editor wants to start with transition and effect; senior starts with light and composition.

Implication: brand with well-directed photography produces well-directed video almost automatically. Brand without photographic direction produces decorated video, not directed.

## 3. The 7 canonical lighting setups

### 3.1 — Golden Hour

**What it is:** natural light of the golden hour (60-90 min after sunrise or before sunset). Warm light, low angle, long soft shadows, atmosphere with slight haze.

**Technical characteristics:**
- Temperature: 3000-3500K (warm)
- Direction: low lateral (sun on horizon)
- Shadows: long, soft, golden
- Atmosphere: subtle haze, visible particles

**When to use:**
- Pieces that evoke intimacy, human warmth, nostalgia
- Aspirational lifestyle (Patagonia, Aesop outdoor)
- Founder portrait, human cases
- Outdoor/nature product

**When NOT to use:**
- Serious, technical, urgent pieces
- Digital/tech product (comes out amateur)
- Crisis communication

**How to describe in an AI prompt:**
> "Lighting: golden hour, sun is low (~30 minutes before sunset), warm amber light from camera left, long soft shadows, golden rim light on subject's hair/edges, slightly diffused atmosphere with subtle haze, color temperature 3000-3500K"

**Master references:**
- Joel Meyerowitz, *Cape Light* (1978)
- Annie Leibovitz, outdoor portraits
- Lars Tunbjörk, Swedish photographer
- Peter Lindbergh, outdoor fashion

---

### 3.2 — Low Key (Chiaroscuro)

**What it is:** most of the frame dark, with small areas of light revealing the subject. Pictorial tradition of Caravaggio, Vermeer, Rembrandt.

**Technical characteristics:**
- Temperature: 4500K (neutral)
- Direction: lateral, frequently Rembrandt (45° upper)
- Shadows: deep, defined
- Blacks: deep (preserve subtle detail)
- Highlights: contained, not blown out

**When to use:**
- Serious editorial portrait (CEO, author, intellectual)
- Premium product (jewel, instrument, single-malt)
- Serious magazine cover (The New Yorker, n+1)
- Pieces of revelation, mystery, contained authority

**When NOT to use:**
- Casual products, joyful lifestyle
- Mass e-commerce
- Playful brand

**How to describe in prompt:**
> "Lighting: low key dramatic chiaroscuro, single hard light source from camera right at 45° angle (Rembrandt lighting), 90% of frame in deep shadow, only subject's face/key elements illuminated, deep blacks throughout but preserving shadow detail, painterly quality reminiscent of Vermeer or Caravaggio, color temperature 4500K (neutral)"

**Master references:**
- Yousuf Karsh, portrait (Churchill 1941)
- Irving Penn, studio portraits
- Paolo Roversi, polaroid 8x10
- Rembrandt as painter (always reference)

---

### 3.3 — Spotlight

**What it is:** light focus isolating ONE element against neutral/dark background. Stage light. Maximum attention, minimum context.

**Technical characteristics:**
- Temperature: 3200K (warm tungsten) or 5500K (daylight) depending on mood
- Direction: frequently from above and slightly frontal
- Beam: ~20° (tight), with fast falloff to deep shadow around
- Visible particles in the beam (theatrical effect)

**When to use:**
- Product launch (absolute focus on the item)
- Portrait with theatrical presence
- Campaign hero shot
- Pieces of "this is the product" — total attention

**When NOT to use:**
- Natural lifestyle
- Documentary
- When you need to show context

**How to describe in prompt:**
> "Lighting: theatrical spotlight from above and slightly forward, tight beam (~20°) hitting subject directly, surrounding falloff to deep shadow within 1-2 meters, dust particles visible in light beam, color temperature 3200K (warm tungsten), product/subject as singular focal point against dark surroundings"

**Master references:**
- Hiro (Yasuhiro Wakabayashi), advertising still life
- Annie Leibovitz, Vanity Fair cover portrait
- Coen Brothers cinema (No Country for Old Men)

---

### 3.4 — Cutter Lights (light cut by pattern)

**What it is:** light cut by gobo, blinds, geometric pattern. Creates visual patterns on subject/background. Modern cinematic style.

**Technical characteristics:**
- Temperature: 4000-5500K depending on mood
- Direction: frequently strong lateral
- Patterns: stripes (blinds), foliage, mesh
- High contrast between lit and unlit areas

**When to use:**
- Stylized editorial fashion
- Contemporary brand with graphic bias
- Music/artists
- Music video, album cover
- Premium product with visual narrative

**When NOT to use:**
- Serious corporate portrait
- Functional e-commerce
- Technical/B2B product

**How to describe in prompt:**
> "Lighting: cutter lights creating geometric patterns, hard light source through Venetian blinds creating strong striped shadows across subject, alternating bright/dark bands, high contrast, color temperature 4000K (slightly warm neutral), film noir editorial aesthetic"

**Master references:**
- Hiroshi Sugimoto (architectural light)
- Cinematography of noir films
- 80s music aesthetic (album art)
- Mert & Marcus, contemporary fashion

---

### 3.5 — Hard Flash (Direct on-camera)

**What it is:** direct flash on the camera, without diffusion. Paparazzi aesthetic, raw fashion, contemporary documentary.

**Technical characteristics:**
- Temperature: 5500K (cold white)
- Direction: frontal direct from camera
- Shadow: hard, defined behind subject
- Highlights: slightly blown on closest features
- Atmosphere: immediate, raw, without deliberate technical refinement

**When to use:**
- Behind-the-scenes
- Irreverent editorial fashion
- Live event (party, show)
- Young irreverent brand (Liquid Death, Brain Dead)
- "Real" content, deliberately without curated aesthetic

**When NOT to use:**
- Serious portrait
- Premium product
- Classic/conservative brand

**How to describe in prompt:**
> "Lighting: hard direct on-camera flash, harsh shadow behind subject, slight overexposure on closest features, color temperature 5500K (cold white), fashion editorial Terry Richardson / Juergen Teller aesthetic, slight motion blur acceptable"

**Master references:**
- Terry Richardson (personally controversial, recognizable technique)
- Juergen Teller, German fashion
- Wolfgang Tillmans
- 2010s Vice Magazine photography

---

### 3.6 — Silhouette (Strong backlight)

**What it is:** subject in black shadow against illuminated background. Form without detail, narrative by outline. Drama through extreme contrast.

**Technical characteristics:**
- Temperature: 5500K (daylight) often
- Direction: strong frontal backlight
- Exposure: for the background, leaves subject completely dark
- Shadow: total on the subject (no detail)
- Edge: rim of light on contours

**When to use:**
- Symbolic, conceptual pieces
- Contemplative cover
- Message about identity/anonymity/transformation
- Reflective scenes

**When NOT to use:**
- When you need to identify the subject
- Product (silhouette hides the product)
- Fashion (hides clothing detail)

**How to describe in prompt:**
> "Lighting: silhouette composition, subject in front of bright window or sky, exposing for the bright background (subject becomes pure black silhouette), strong rim of light on subject's edges, no detail in shadow, color temperature 5500K (daylight), contemplative meditative mood"

**Master references:**
- Steve McCurry, documentary photography
- Andrei Tarkovsky cinema
- Harry Gruyaert (contrasted color photography)

---

### 3.7 — Soft Window Light (Vermeer)

**What it is:** diffused natural light entering through a large window (preferably north-facing, in the northern hemisphere; south-facing, in the southern hemisphere — consistent indirect light).

**Technical characteristics:**
- Temperature: 5500-6500K (daylight, slightly cool)
- Direction: soft lateral
- Quality: diffused (color wraps around)
- Shadows: soft, with gradual falloff
- Atmosphere: contemplative, patient

**When to use:**
- Sober editorial portrait
- Premium product still life
- Contemplative interior lifestyle (interior magazine)
- Person in domestic environment
- Food (elevated restaurant photo)

**When NOT to use:**
- Drama, urgency
- Outdoor scenes
- When you need strong contrast

**How to describe in prompt:**
> "Lighting: soft natural window light from camera left (north-facing window or equivalent), wraps subject in gentle even illumination, soft falloff into mid shadows on right side, painterly Vermeer quality, color temperature 5500K (daylight), contemplative interior atmosphere"

**Master references:**
- Vermeer (painting, always reference)
- Saul Leiter
- Rinko Kawauchi
- Photography from The Gentlewoman, Apartamento

## 4. Composition — consolidated frameworks

### 4.1 — Rule of thirds

Divides frame into 9 parts (3×3). Main points of interest at the 4 crossings (intersections). Dividing lines help align horizon, axes.

**When to use:** most photos. Safe default.
**When to ignore:** deliberate symmetry (Wes Anderson) or bold crop.

### 4.2 — Centralized symmetry

Subject centralized, balanced composition side by side. Carries authority, formality, monumentality.

**When to use:** confrontational portrait (Avedon), architecture, conservative/luxury brand.
**When NOT to use:** natural lifestyle (looks artificial).

### 4.3 — Leading lines

Lines within the frame (road, wall, table) that lead the eye to the subject. Visual depth.

**When to use:** wide shot with depth narrative.
**When NOT to use:** product closeup (irrelevant).

### 4.4 — Foreground/middle/background

Composition in 3 planes. Out-of-focus foreground to give context, in-focus subject in middle, out-of-focus background for atmosphere.

**When to use:** cinematic photo, premium lifestyle.
**Equipment needed:** lens with shallow DOF (35mm f/1.4, 50mm f/1.4 or similar).

### 4.5 — Negative space

Small subject in the frame, large empty area. Communicates isolation, contemplation, importance (paradoxically).

**When to use:** editorial cover, conceptual hero shot, meditative piece.
**Reference aesthetic:** Wallpaper magazine, Apartamento.

### 4.6 — Frame within frame

Subject framed by secondary element (door, window, arch). Creates focus and context simultaneously.

**When to use:** portrait in environment, travel photography, narrative.

### 4.7 — Camera positions

| Angle | Communicates |
|---|---|
| **Eye-level** | Neutrality, direct connection |
| **Low angle** (from below) | Authority, monumentality — dangerous in portrait (apparent chin) |
| **High angle** (from above) | Vulnerability, observation — dangerous in portrait (infantile effect) |
| **Overhead 90°** | Flat lay, still life, gastronomic editorial |
| **Dutch angle** (oblique) | Tension, instability — use sparingly |

## 5. Color treatment (color grading)

### 5.1 — Fundamental decisions

| Axis | Poles |
|---|---|
| **Saturation** | Naturalist / Desaturated / Selective saturated (1 vivid color, rest subtle) / Total saturated |
| **Temperature** | Warm (3000-3500K) / Neutral (4500-5500K) / Cold (6000K+) |
| **Contrast** | Low (matte film) / Medium / High (sharp digital) |
| **Blacks** | Deep (#000) / Raised (#1A1A1A — matte/film effect) |
| **Highlights** | Preserved / Blown out (bleach) |
| **Grain** | Absent / Subtle / Present / Simulated organic |
| **Halation/bloom** | Absent / Subtle on lights / Present |

### 5.2 — Selective saturation (technique)

Recurring principle in sophisticated editorial photography:

- Desaturated background (grays, browns, off-whites)
- 1 high saturation point in the brand's primary color
- Eye goes straight to the saturated point — visual direction without text

Application: product in primary color + neutral environment = instant visual identity.

### 5.3 — Reference cinematic color grades

| Grade | Predominant color | Mood | Reference |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hollywood Teal & Orange** | Teal in shadows + orange on skin | Mainstream cinema | Michael Bay, Marvel |
| **Wong Kar-wai green-blue** | Saturated greenish-blue | Urban loneliness, melancholy | *In the Mood for Love* |
| **Roger Deakins amber** | Dense amber | Nostalgia, historical warmth | *1917*, *Skyfall* |
| **Wes Anderson pastel** | Symmetric saturated pastel | Playful, controlled | *Grand Budapest Hotel* |
| **Vivian Maier sat** | 60s saturated color | Urban documentary | personal work |
| **Vermeer naturalism** | Neutral naturalism | Contemplative, timeless | *Girl with a Pearl Earring* |
| **David Fincher digital sharp** | Desaturated green-yellow | Contemporary somber | *Se7en*, *Mindhunter* |

### 5.4 — Anti-color grading

- ❌ Saturated Instagram filters (Lo-fi, Valencia, etc.) — dated, amateur
- ❌ Exaggerated HDR (halos, supersaturation) — questionable taste
- ❌ Strong vignette (>15%) — dated
- ❌ "Hollywood teal & orange" color grading without narrative purpose (generic)
- ❌ "Instant" B&W (preset button) — intentional black and white requires worked curve
- ❌ Exaggerated sharpening (visible artifacts)
- ❌ Clarity slider at maximum (artificial texture)

## 6. Directing people

### 6.1 — Canonical types

Every brand decides who appears in its photography. Strategic decision:

| Axis | Poles |
|---|---|
| **Age range** | Young (20-30) / Adult (30-50) / Senior (50+) / Intergenerational mix |
| **Ethnicity/diversity** | Mirrors persona / Mirrors expanded audience / Tokenized diversity (caution) |
| **Style** | Casual / Professional / Artistic / Urban / Rural |
| **Wardrobe** | Neutral palette / Brand palette / Free style / Uniform |
| **Body status** | Active / Contemplative / Work / Leisure |

### 6.2 — Posture and expression

| ✅ Allowed | ❌ Forbidden |
|---|---|
| Natural, captured in action | Forced catalog smile |
| Posed but without rigidity | Corporate "crossed arms" pose |
| Direct gaze to camera (Avedon) — if that's the style | Artificial "thoughtful" gaze |
| Contained expression | Open laugh without reason |
| Real gesture (hand touching object) | Generic handshake |
| Gaze outside the frame (contemplative) | "Lost in the horizon" cliché gaze |

### 6.3 — Subtle direction

Good director of people:
- Talks before photographing (relaxes the subject)
- Asks for real action ("tell a story", "tell an inside joke") instead of pose
- Captures between poses, not in the poses
- Works in series (50+ photos to choose 1)

### 6.4 — Anti-direction (forbidden)

- ❌ Forced "smile at the camera"
- ❌ Hand on hip 90s lookbook style
- ❌ Fixed gaze at camera + wide smile (Getty catalog)
- ❌ Obvious "diverse" model (one of each ethnicity, done) — tokenism
- ❌ Entire team smiling in the meeting room
- ❌ Symbolic handshake

## 7. Directing setting and objects

### 7.1 — Canonical settings by mood

| Mood | Settings |
|---|---|
| **Intimate domestic** | Kitchen with natural light, living room with books, breakfast table |
| **Sober professional** | Neutral studio, minimalist office, room with plants |
| **Aspirational outdoor** | Golden hour beach, mountain, outdoor café |
| **Contemporary urban** | Street with wet asphalt, metropolitan café, public transport |
| **Authorial industrial** | Warehouse with lateral light, workshop with tools, studio |
| **Sophisticated editorial** | Art gallery, boutique hotel, intimate restaurant |

### 7.2 — Objects as protagonists

Brands with strong visual direction frequently develop **vocabulary of recurring objects**:
- **Aesop** — amber flask, marble, natural stone
- **Patagonia** — real outdoor equipment, marks of use
- **Apple** — product isolated on neutral background
- **Apartamento** — personal objects loaded with history

The brand can define its "props":
- Which object appears frequently
- Which carry brand meaning
- Which become visual identity over time

### 7.3 — Forbidden settings

- ❌ Generic corporate office (with PC tower)
- ❌ Meeting room with whiteboard
- ❌ Generic Starbucks-like coffee shop
- ❌ Saturated coworking space
- ❌ Studio with infinite white background without purpose
- ❌ Background pattern (brick wall, plant wall, etc.)

## 8. Video / motion direction

### 8.1 — Universal principles (applied cinematography)

Video is photography in motion. Principles:

- **Shot before movement** — well-composed static camera beats badly executed pan/tilt
- **Light first, action after** — if the light is right, the rest adjusts
- **Editing reveals story** — cut is narrative decision, not technical
- **Sound design carries 50% of the emotion** — ambient sound, foley, music, voice
- **Color grading consistency** — all shots pass through the same grade

### 8.2 — Fundamental decisions by brand

| Aspect | Poles |
|---|---|
| **Editing rhythm** | Slow contemplative (Wes Anderson, A24) / Medium editorial (documentary) / Fast cut (pop advertising) |
| **Cuts** | Dry (jump cuts) / Subtle cross-dissolve / Match cuts |
| **Camera movement** | Static / Subtle pan/tilt / Steadicam / Handheld documentary |
| **Aspect ratio** | 16:9 (classic cinema) / 4:5 (Instagram feed) / 9:16 (Reels/TikTok) / 21:9 (extreme cinematic) |
| **On-screen text** | Absent / Simple caption / Kinetic typography |
| **Music** | Original / Curated library / Silence with ambient sound |
| **Voice-over** | Absent / Conversational present / Institutional present |

### 8.3 — Transitions

**Allowed (with purpose):**
- ✅ Dry cut (professional default)
- ✅ Subtle cross-dissolve (time transition)
- ✅ Match cut (visual or audio connecting shots)
- ✅ J-cut / L-cut (audio leads or continues) — serious editorial resource

**Forbidden:**
- ❌ Dramatic zoom (dated)
- ❌ Spin / Rotation (dated)
- ❌ Wipe (Star Wars, rarely justifiable)
- ❌ Glitch transition (2020-2023 TikTok cliché)
- ❌ Automatic CapCut/Premiere transitions (zoom, blur, slide with effect) — amateur
- ❌ Whoosh sound on every cut

### 8.4 — Sound design

Sound layers:
1. **Dialogue / main voice**
2. **Music**
3. **Ambient sound** (room tone, exterior)
4. **Foley** (specific sounds: footsteps, glass on table)
5. **Sound effects** (motion design, transitions)

Premium brand invests in all 5. Amateur brand ignores 3-5 and ends up with "weird" video without knowing why (usually it's lack of room tone + foley).

### 8.5 — Voice-over

- **Who narrates?** Founder (intimate), professional voice actor (institutional), AI voice (caution — synthetic voice detectable and amateur in serious piece)
- **When to use?** Storytelling, product explanation, historical context
- **When NOT to use?** Organic pieces (visual Reels), conceptual art

## 10. What is FAST vs SLOW photo (efficiency)

### 10.1 — FAST (scalable)

- **Curated stock photo** (Death to Stock, Stocksy, Twenty20) — when well selected, saves time
- **Series photo session** — 1 day of production, 100+ photos to use for 6 months
- **Treatment templates** (LUTs, Lightroom presets) — automatic applied color grade
- **Generative AI** (Higgsfield CLI + Nano Banana 2) — for contexts that don't need real photo
- **Monthly retainer photographer** — continuous relationship, direct brief
- **Own organized and searchable image bank**
- **iPhone photo with natural light** — 80% of the work for informal pieces

### 10.2 — SLOW (artisanal, worth it in specific cases)

- **Single photo session per piece** — expensive, slow, worth it in hero campaign
- **Custom location + set design** — worth it in big campaign, not daily post
- **Pixel-perfect post-production** — worth it in book cover, not story
- **Polaroid or analog film** — sought aesthetic, but slow process
- **Approval from 5 stakeholders** — kills creative production

### 10.3 — Photographic efficiency: golden rule

> Brand that produces 4-8 pieces/month needs photography in volume — monthly session + organized bank + AI for fill.
>
> Brand that produces 1 hero/campaign per quarter is worth a dedicated photographer per session.
>
> Brand that produces 30+ pieces/month needs a SYSTEM: templates, presets, huge bank, contracts with multiple photographers.

## 11. What SELLS vs REPELS (commercial photography)

### 11.1 — SELLS (tested in e-commerce, lifestyle, beauty)

**Fashion e-commerce:**
- Product photo on neutral background (white/off-white) — primary
- Model in natural movement (not rigid pose)
- Details of fabric, stitching, finish (close-ups)
- Lifestyle context (product in real use)
- 360° photo or multi-angle carousel
- Short video (5-15s) of product worn in movement
- Model of diverse size (real bodies)

**Beauty/cosmetic:**
- Product texture in close (cream applied on real hand)
- Real result (honest before/after, not exaggerated)
- Application on face with real-skin model (visible pores)
- True color of the product (without editing to look more saturated)

**Food/restaurant:**
- Lateral natural light (not on-camera flash)
- Food on real plate with cutlery (not excessively styled)
- Visible human hands (serving, eating)
- Restaurant environment in background (authenticity)

**Tech/SaaS:**
- Real product screenshot (not fake mockup)
- Person using in real context (not posing with laptop)
- Well-photographed UI detail
- Visualized technical architecture

### 11.2 — REPELS (tested, drops conversion)

**General:**
- Identifiable stock photo (immediate loss of credibility)
- Model posing artificially
- Over-edited photo (plastic skin, super-white teeth)
- Strange angle that distorts product
- Lack of context (loose product without reference)

**Fashion:**
- Single model (always young white woman) — market loss
- Photo only on white background without lifestyle (without use context)
- Editing that misrepresents real color of the product

**Beauty:**
- Before/after with aggressive editing (generates distrust and legal actions)
- Model with unreal perfect skin (young generation detects)

**Food:**
- Food with direct flash (looks amateur)
- Supersaturated colors (looks artificial, not appetizing)
- Complicated composition (exaggerated 90s food styling)

**Tech:**
- Fake phone screen mockup (detectable)
- Screenshot with invented text / lorem ipsum (loss of credibility)
- "Happy" person using product without showing the product

## 13. Exhaustive anti-photography

List of EVERYTHING that NEVER appears in well-done brand photography.

### Composition and framing
- ❌ Person with head cut off (without artistic purpose)
- ❌ Crop at joints (knee, elbow, wrist)
- ❌ Rigid centralization in corporate portrait
- ❌ Medium shot (waist up) dated
- ❌ Low camera in portrait (apparent chin, unreal)
- ❌ Messy background competing with subject

### Light
- ❌ Direct on-camera flash in serious piece
- ❌ Green/yellow fluorescent light (90s office)
- ❌ Mixed light without correction (warm + cool in the same frame)
- ❌ Hard shadows without purpose
- ❌ Backlit subject without fill, burned

### Color
- ❌ Saturated Instagram filter
- ❌ Exaggerated HDR (halos)
- ❌ Strong vignette (>15%)
- ❌ Generic "Hollywood teal & orange" color grading
- ❌ Instant B&W without curve work
- ❌ Exaggerated sharpening

### People
- ❌ Forced catalog smile
- ❌ Corporate handshake
- ❌ "Thoughtful" person with hand on chin
- ❌ Tokenized "diverse" posing model
- ❌ Entire team smiling in the meeting room
- ❌ Artificial "lost in the horizon" gaze
- ❌ 90s lookbook pose (hand on hip)

### Settings
- ❌ Generic corporate office
- ❌ Meeting room with whiteboard
- ❌ Generic coffee shop
- ❌ Background pattern (brick wall cliché)
- ❌ Infinite white studio without purpose

### Conceptual clichés
- ❌ Lit lamp as idea
- ❌ Gears as strategy
- ❌ Jigsaw with missing piece as solution
- ❌ Hand holding lamp/clock/money
- ❌ Photo of keyboard with chart on monitor

### Video
- ❌ Drone shot aerial without purpose
- ❌ City time lapse at sunset
- ❌ B-roll of "code rolling on screen" for tech context
- ❌ Gratuitous slow-motion
- ❌ Glitch transitions
- ❌ Auto-playing video with sound

- ❌ AI faces with glazed eyes or strange symmetry
- ❌ Hands with 6 fingers
- ❌ Incoherent text on signs/posters
- ❌ Inconsistent shadows (light in contradictory direction)
- ❌ Physically impossible reflections

## 14. Quality criteria

### 14.1 — The 7 criteria

1. **Clear direction** — does the photo communicate something intentional? Was it not chance?
2. **Controlled light** — direction, quality, temperature coherent?
3. **Functional composition** — clear visual hierarchy?
4. **Intentional focus** — DOF, focal point correct?
5. **Coherent color grade** — passes through the brand's color system?
6. **Authentic moment** — real people/action, not artificially posed?
7. **Memory** — distinctive photo, would you remember it 1 month from now?

5+ "yes" = professional photo. 7/7 = exceptional photo.

### 14.2 — Brand identity test

Mix 1 photo of the brand with 8 photos of competing brands. Distinguishable? If it disappears, failed. If it positively draws attention, worked.

### 14.3 — Thumbnail test

Reduce photo to 200×200px. Still communicates? If it becomes a blob, composition failed.
