## 0. Doctrine

The biggest difference between a mediocre brand and a remembered brand is the copy. Logo in some cases, visual identity in others — but what touches every touchpoint, always, is the word. Transactional email. Caption. Error message. Headline. Bio. Reply to comment.

A brand's copy does not have the mission of "being beautiful" or "being persuasive". It has the mission of **being unmistakable**. You close your eyes reading a strong brand's text and you know who it is. Ogilvy wrote: *"If you can't write the best copy in the world, hire someone who can. The selection of words is more important than any other single factor in the success of an advertisement."* Replace "advertisement" with "brand" and the rule remains valid in 2026.

Every decision in this document starts from a premise: **writing for a brand is a technical discipline, not a gift**. There is canon. There are rules. There are anti-patterns. And whoever says "my brand has its own voice, doesn't fit in a framework" usually has a weak voice disguised in mysticism.

## 1. Reference literature

Every decision in this document is anchored in canonical works. If there's doubt about a rule, consult these sources first — they are the court of appeal.

### Prose foundations

- **Strunk & White** — *The Elements of Style* (1959). The most influential document about prose in modern English. Rules: omit needless words; use the active voice; use definite, specific, concrete language. Applicable to Portuguese with minimal adaptation.
- **William Zinsser** — *On Writing Well* (1976). Clarity manifesto. Central principle: "the secret of good writing is to strip every sentence to its cleanest components".
- **Verlyn Klinkenborg** — *Several Short Sentences About Writing* (2012). Argues against "flow" and in favor of the short sentence as unit. Reads in an afternoon, recalibrates life.
- **Mary Norris** — *Between You & Me* (2015). Editorial rigor by The New Yorker's senior copy editor. Living grammar.
- **Roy Peter Clark** — *Writing Tools* (2006). 50 practical tools. Direct application.
- **Steven Pinker** — *The Sense of Style* (2014). Cognitive linguistics applied to writing.

### Commercial and brand copy

- **David Ogilvy** — *Ogilvy on Advertising* (1983) and *Confessions of an Advertising Man* (1963). Lasting principles about headlines, body copy, and the morality of persuasion.
- **John Caples** — *Tested Advertising Methods* (1932). The bible of the testable headline. Prior to the digital era, still absurdly valid.
- **Bill Bernbach** — speeches and essays collected in *Bill Bernbach Said* (Doyle Dane Bernbach). The creative revolution of the 60s.
- **George Lois** — *Damn Good Advice (For People with Talent!)* (2012). Editorial daring in the form of maxims.
- **Joanna Wiebe** — Copyhackers (digital collection). Voice-of-customer copy, sales page formulas based on qualitative data.

## 2. The 12 founding principles

Non-negotiable doctrine. All copy passes through them. Failure in any one = rewrite.

### Principle 1 — Specific beats generic, always

The biggest weakness of Brazilian brand copy is the generic. "Innovation", "transformation", "solutions", "quality" — words that fit any company, therefore define none. The rule: replace the subject of the sentence with another subject. If the sentence still makes sense, it's generic. Rewrite until the sentence only fits your brand.

> Generic: "Companies are adopting new technologies to improve results."
>
> Specific: "[Concrete company] has [specific number] of users and [concrete metric] because [causal mechanism] reduces [measurable problem] by [%]."

Theoretical anchoring: Strunk rule 17 ("omit needless words"); Zinsser ch. 6 ("Words"); Tufte ("show the data").

### Principle 2 — Proper name carries more weight than adjective

Say "Patagonia" instead of "sustainable outdoor brand". Say "Stratechery" instead of "technology newsletter". A proper name triggers memory and category simultaneously. An adjective only triggers category.

In practice: in a Brazilian brand piece, citing real references (even from other niches) makes copy automatically gain depth. "We sell like an Apple" is sterile; "we sell like Aesop sells — product as ritual, not commodity" is specific.

### Principle 3 — Short sentence is unit. Not flow.

Klinkenborg treats the sentence as **fundamental unit of thought**, not as a piece of flow. Each sentence should be able to exist alone. If a sentence only makes sense leaning on the previous one, perhaps it's the previous one that's incomplete.

Practical implication: when in doubt between 1 sentence of 25 words with 3 commas, or 2 sentences of 12 words with 1 period each — choose the 2 sentences. Always.

> Worse: "Neymar launches brand while he's sidelined, and the brand is born as a category built from the accessory the public recognizes."
>
> Better: "Neymar launches the brand while he's sidelined. The brand is born as a clear continuation of the personality the public recognizes."

### Principle 4 — Strong verb always beats adverb

"Walked quickly" is weakness. "Ran" is strength. Stephen King wrote: *"The road to hell is paved with adverbs."* Also valid for Portuguese. Every time you want to write an adverb, ask if there's a verb that carries that adverb embedded.

List of underused expressive Brazilian verbs: launch, plant, drive, knock down, scratch, cut, throw, release, hold, turn, fold, stretch, compress, break.

### Principle 5 — Active voice, with few exceptions

Strunk rule 14. Passive voice weakens, distances, hides subject. Active voice names the agent, attributes responsibility, generates energy.

> Passive: "The campaign was created by the marketing team in 2024."
>
> Active: "The marketing team created the campaign in 2024."

Valid exception: when the agent is irrelevant or when you deliberately want to hide the agent (rare in brand copy).

### Principle 6 — Natural connective sustains fluidity

Brazilian Portuguese has rich colloquial connectives that sustain fluidity without becoming formal: **because, only that, therefore, while, when, but, then, so, even so, still**.

A choppy paragraph = stalled reading. Each copy block should function as a **reporting paragraph** — subject, verb, complement and at least one connective between sentences.

> Choppy: "Sales is execution. Performance matters. Result defines career."
>
> Fluid: "Sales is individual execution, because face-to-face performance is what counts when the result needs to show up."

### Principle 7 — Show, don't tell

Canonical principle of literature, equally valid in copy. Don't say your brand is "innovative" — show what it does that no one does. Don't say your brand is "premium" — show the level of finish.

> Telling: "We are a premium brand that values every detail."
>
> Showing: "Each box leaves our atelier wrapped in Japanese wax paper, with the ribbon tied by us. We take 4 minutes per box."

### Principle 8 — Hooks anchored in fact, not in rhetorical question

Rhetorical question as opening is a Brazilian TED talk vice. "Have you ever stopped to think...?" and variants died in 2018. Replace with: dated fact + tension.

> Vice: "Have you ever stopped to think how Instagram has changed in the last 5 years?"
>
> Alive: "In February 2026, Instagram dropped organic reach by 23% for accounts with less than 10k. It was predictable. What no one predicted is that..."

Modern hook structure: **[Dated fact] + [Negation of obvious cause] + [Pointing to real cause]**.

### Principle 9 — Closure pivots, doesn't summarize

Weak copy ends by summarizing what it said. Strong copy ends by opening a new layer of implication at the exact moment it ends. The pivot is what makes the reader think after closing the tab.

> Summarizes (weak): "In the end, what the data shows is that the right topic makes all the difference in content creation."
>
> Pivots (strong): "The 1,168 posts prove that distribution and quality don't compete — but distribution comes first. Whoever ignores this produces for the bubble, not for growth."

### Principle 10 — Data is worth more than opinion, whenever possible

Number, source, year. Without that, it's opinion. Opinion is OK in an authorial essay; in brand copy, without data it's wind.

Absolute rule: **number + source + year**. "Most people..." doesn't count. "62% of Brazilians between 25-34, according to PNAD 2023..." counts.

### Principle 11 — Brand copy is edited, not written

Anne Lamott called it "shitty first drafts". All good copy passes through 3-7 passes of editing. First draft serves to have material; from the second on is where copy is born.

Cutting is more important than adding. Hemingway revised *A Farewell to Arms* 47 times. The central editorial question: "Is this word/sentence/paragraph doing work, or is it filling space?"

### Principle 12 — The brand never crosses the tone

The brand can modulate tone (serious vs. intimate vs. commercial vs. crisis), but the **personality doesn't change**. A reader who knows the brand's voice through a carousel cover should recognize the same voice in the transactional email, in the reply to a comment, in the ad. If they don't recognize, the brand has crossed the tone.

The mirror rule: imagine a loyal customer of the brand simultaneously receiving a caption + an email + a reply to DM. Were the three written by the same person? If the answer is "no", the brand crossed.

## 3. The difference between Voice and Tone

Confusion between voice and tone is universal. They are not synonyms.

| Aspect | **Voice** | **Tone** |
|---|---|---|
| **What it is** | Brand's fixed personality. Doesn't change. | Contextual modulation of voice. Changes. |
| **When it changes** | Rarely (rebrand, strategic repositioning) | Every piece (channel + intention combination) |
| **Comparison** | How a specific person speaks (their style) | How they speak in different situations (sarcastic at dinner with friends, serious in job interview) |
| **Voice Example** | "Direct, slightly acidic, culturally informed, with editorial criterion" | — |
| **Tone Example** | — | "In transactional email: factual and short. In Instagram caption: acidic and imagery. In crisis: institutional and assumed." |

Voice is fixed across the entire DNA. Tone is modulated in **5-7 typical contexts** (mapped in DNA.md, section 4.5 and detail in 4.7-4.13).

The operational definition of tone is a matrix of variables:
- Formality (formal ↔ casual)
- Distance (distant ↔ close)
- Irony (sincere ↔ ironic)
- Density (essayistic ↔ telegraphic)
- Urgency (calm ↔ urgent)

Each context positions itself in this matrix. Voice is the gravity point around which the matrix oscillates.

Reference: Mailchimp Content Style Guide ("Voice and Tone" section) is the best public systematization of this model.

## 4. Consolidated frameworks (with attribution)

### 4.1 — Voice Pillars Framework (Heath & Heath, *Made to Stick*)

Every strong brand has 3-5 voice pillars that sustain verbal identity. They are not loose adjectives — they are articulated verbal principles.

Example (fictional editorial brand):
1. **Rigor over charm.** We prefer uncomfortable truth to reassuring charm.
2. **Curiosity over certainty.** We mark hypothesis, not verdict.
3. **Specific over generic.** Every assertion carries example.
4. **Restraint over exuberance.** Short sentence beats rich sentence.

Each pillar is a **verbal value translated into editorial practice**. Adjective is symptom; pillar is principle.

### 4.2 — Tone Modulation Matrix (Atlassian Design)

4D matrix to map how tone varies by context:

| Axis | Pole A | Pole B |
|---|---|---|
| Formality | Formal | Casual |
| Energy | Calm | Urgent |
| Status | Serious | Playful |
| Engagement | Direct | Inviting |

Each brand touchpoint receives a position (1-5) on each axis. Tone of transactional email = (Formal 4, Calm 4, Serious 4, Direct 5). Tone of Instagram caption = (Casual 4, Calm 3, Playful 2, Inviting 4).

Result: matrix of 4 dimensions × N touchpoints. Operates as **tonal compass**.

### 4.3 — BRAND-SCRIPT (Donald Miller, *Building a StoryBrand*)

7-element framework for structuring a brand message:

1. **Character** — who the customer is (not the brand)
2. **Problem** — the problem they have (3 levels: external, internal, philosophical)
3. **Guide** — the brand as guide (not hero)
4. **Plan** — clear steps to solve
5. **Call to action** — directive, not invitation
6. **Failure** — what happens if they don't act
7. **Success** — transformation if they act

Useful mainly in sales page, ad, institutional pitch. Less useful in editorial caption.

### 4.4 — PAS (Problem, Agitate, Solution) — classic direct copy

Structure tested in direct sales copy since the 1920s. 3 movements:

1. **Problem** — names the pain
2. **Agitate** — deepens the pain (consequences, emotion)
3. **Solution** — presents the solution

Modern version avoids the manipulative "agitate" (generating artificial anxiety) and transforms into **Problem → Mechanism → Resolution** — names the problem, explains the underlying mechanism, offers resolution. More ethical, more sophisticated.

### 4.5 — AIDA (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action)

The oldest direct copy framework (attributed to Elias St. Elmo Lewis, 1898). Remains base of all sales copy in short format (ad, headline, CTA).

- **Attention** — capture (hook)
- **Interest** — sustain (mechanism, context)
- **Desire** — awaken (specific benefit)
- **Action** — convert (unequivocal CTA)

In contemporary brand copy, AIDA works more as diagnosis ("which element of this piece failed?") than as recipe.

### 4.6 — Hook → Mechanism → Proof → Pivot (modern editorial structure)

Structure derived from journalism + contemporary sales copy. Particularly effective in carousel format, long post, editorial newsletter.

1. **Hook** — dated fact + tension (Principle 8)
2. **Mechanism** — the underlying "why" (what causes the fact)
3. **Proof** — concrete data that anchors
4. **Pivot** — thematic turn that opens a new layer (Principle 9)

Each movement has approximate textual weight: Hook 15%, Mechanism 35%, Proof 30%, Pivot 20%.

### 4.7 — Voice Spectrum (Mailchimp Content Style Guide)

For each text, position on 4 spectrums:

| Spectrum | Pole A | Pole B |
|---|---|---|
| Voice | Serious | Playful |
| Voice | Formal | Casual |
| Voice | Respectful | Irreverent |
| Voice | Enthusiastic | Pragmatic |

Different from the Tone Modulation Matrix (which changes by context), the Voice Spectrum describes **the brand's anchor position**. It can oscillate slightly, but doesn't completely cross poles.

### 4.8 — VOC Copy (Joanna Wiebe, Copyhackers)

Voice-of-customer copy: the most persuasive copy is the one that **uses the customers' exact words**, not the marketers' words.

Method:
1. Collection of interview transcripts, reviews, comments, DMs
2. Mapping of **textual phrases** that repeat
3. Substitution of internal copy with real phrases from the audience
4. Comparative testing

When the brand has no audience yet (in ideation or construction), VOC is replaced by **construction of linguistic protagonist** — a linguistic persona synthesized from cultural references that the hypothetical audience consumes.

## 5. Anatomy of brand prose

### 5.1 — Brand paragraph structure

A well-constructed contemporary Brazilian brand paragraph has:

- **Opening sentence** that makes a claim or presents a fact (12-22 words)
- **2-4 developing sentences** that sustain, exemplify or expand (10-18 words each)
- **Closing sentence** that pivots or implies (8-15 words)

Total: 4-6 sentences, 60-110 words. Paragraphs larger than that provoke abandonment on mobile.

### 5.2 — Hook structure

Every editorial piece needs a hook. Bad hook = piece not read.

Valid structures (in order of strength):

1. **Dated fact + turn** (Principle 8): "On [date], [fact]. It wasn't [obvious explanation]. It was [real explanation]."
2. **Factual contradiction**: "[Brand A] has [result X]. [Brand B] has [opposite result X]. The difference isn't [obvious hypothesis]."
3. **Specific scene**: "[Specific character] is [concrete action] at [place]. [Detail that carries tension]."
4. **Anomalous number**: "[Unexpected high number] [unit]. In [short time]. None of [expected explanation]."
5. **Striking short quote**: "[Phrase in quotes, ≤12 words]. [Who said and when]."

Forbidden hooks:
- ❌ Rhetorical question ("Have you ever stopped to think...")
- ❌ Generic statement ("In a world where...")
- ❌ Encyclopedic definition ("[Topic] is the set of practices...")
- ❌ Non-specific promise ("Everything you need to know about...")

### 5.3 — Closure structure (pivot)

Valid closures:

1. **Amplified implication**: "The [data] shows [direct conclusion]. What no one comments is [bigger implication]."
2. **Reframing**: "[What seemed to be about X] is, deep down, about [Y]. Whoever operates with this lens sees the problem differently."
3. **Distilled principle**: "Whoever [right behavior] [good result]. Whoever [wrong behavior] [bad result]. The rule is simple."
4. **Concrete next step** (in commercial copy): "Next step: [specific action in 5-8 words]."

Forbidden closures:
- ❌ Summary of what came before ("In the end, what the data shows is that...")
- ❌ Rhetorical question without answer ("The question that remains is: will you continue ignoring?")
- ❌ Cinematic speculation ("Somewhere in an office in Brazil, someone is...")
- ❌ Moralizing ("The problem is that people don't understand...")

### 5.4 — CTA structure

A strong CTA has 3 properties:

1. **Specific action verb** (not "learn more", not "click here")
2. **Concrete object** (what, exactly)
3. **Implicit promise of low friction** (because it's worth spending 2 seconds)

Approved templates:
- "Comment [WORD] and receive [concrete thing] in DM"
- "I want [specific result]" (in button)
- "Tag someone who [specific behavior]"
- "Save to [specific future use]"

Forbidden CTAs:
- ❌ "Learn more"
- ❌ "Check it out"
- ❌ "Click here"
- ❌ "Don't miss"
- ❌ "Let's go!"
- ❌ "You need to see this"

## 6. The 7 quality parameters of text

All copy passes through 7 parameters before being approved. Minimum grade 8/10 in each. A parameter below 8 = rewrite.

### Parameter 1 — Grammar and mechanics

**Critical focus in Brazilian Portuguese:** missing articles.

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Right |
|---|---|
| "in period less than 1 year" | "in **a** period less than 1 year" |
| "is generic brand" | "is **a** generic brand" |
| "generation that grew up with smartphone" | "**a** generation that grew up with **the** smartphone" |

Other points:
- Verb agreement: plural subject requires plural verb
- Fragments without verb are forbidden: *"Protein so as not to lose muscle"* is not a sentence
- Correct nominal regency
- Crasis when mandatory

**Penalty:** missing article → max grade 7/10. Wrong agreement → max grade 5/10. Fragment without verb → immediate rewrite.

### Parameter 2 — Fluidity

**Test:** read the block aloud. If it sounded robotic, truncated or seemed like a list disguised as a sentence → failed.

Each block needs to function as a reporting paragraph. Mandatory connectives in use: because, only that, therefore, while, when, but, then, so, even so, still.

**Penalty:** choppy block without connectives → max grade 5/10.

### Parameter 3 — Anti-AI Slop

Exhaustive list of prohibitions below in this document (section 7). Summary:

- Zero binary structures ("not X, but Y")
- Zero tics ("and this changes everything", "in the end")
- Zero corporate jargon when colloquial equivalent exists
- Zero numeric anglicisms ("10+", "5x")
- Zero generic constructions ("in an X way")

**Penalty:** one binary structure → max grade 6/10. One tic → max grade 5/10.

### Parameter 4 — Verified facts

**Absolute rule:** no number, statistic, date, monetary value or quote enters without verification via web search.

Always verify:
- Numbers of users, followers, views
- Survey percentages (source and year)
- Company revenue, valuation
- Quotes attributed to people
- Launch dates

**Standardization:** if a table has precise percentages ("28.6%"), all other fields need to be in the same format. Don't mix with vague ("High").

**Penalty:** unverified data → max grade 6/10.

### Parameter 5 — Structure

Each piece has expected structure. For editorial copy: Hook → Mechanism → Proof → Pivot.

- [ ] Anatomy preserved
- [ ] All hook promises are fulfilled before closure
- [ ] Closure makes genuine pivot (doesn't summarize)

**Most serious error: unfulfilled promise.** If the hook says "three decisions", the content needs to have three decisions.

### Parameter 6 — Density

**Test:** remove all articles, connectives and adjectives. What remains is substance. If vague and generic → rewrite from scratch.

**Warning signs:**
- "Athletes do X" without specific case
- "Brands use Y" without example
- Any block that would work with another subject in its place

**Substitution test:** swap the block's subject for another (runner → photographer). If it still makes sense → it's generic, rewrite.

### Parameter 7 — Editorial tone

**Prefer 2 short sentences with period, avoid 1 long sentence with comma.**

**Allowed resources:**
- Em dash (—) to add context without breaking flow
- Parentheses in colloquial tone
- Strategic anglicisms already incorporated ("views", "craft", "fit")

**Forbidden resources:**
- Excessive formality
- Metalanguage ("the text shows", "this post explains")
- Second person ("you", "your") in the body of editorial pieces — write like reporting, not advice
- **Exception:** second person allowed in direct emails, FAQ, and CTAs

## 7. Exhaustive anti-patterns

List of everything that NEVER appears in well-made brand copy. The Maestro actively audits each piece against this list.

### 7.1 Forced binary constructions (forbidden)

```
❌ "Not X, but Y"
❌ "It was never about X, it's about Y"
❌ "X decreases, Y accelerates"
❌ "While X loses, Y gains"
❌ "Less X, more Y"
❌ "Before: X. Now: Y."
❌ "No X. No Y."
❌ "More than X, less than Y"
```

When contrast is real and necessary, write in prose with natural connector — not in two-point formula.

### 7.2 Forbidden openings

```
❌ "Today we're going to talk about..."
❌ "In this post you'll learn..."
❌ "Before we start..."
❌ "As you probably know..."
❌ "Many people ask..."
❌ "Everyone has heard of..."
❌ "Have you ever stopped to think..."
❌ "In a world where..."
❌ "We live in an era where..."
```

A piece opens on the fact, on the tension or on the data. Never on the preparation.

### 7.3 Forbidden headlines

```
❌ "When X becomes Y"
❌ "The rise of X"
❌ "The impact of X"
❌ "Why X is changing Y"
❌ "X: what you need to know"
❌ "Everything you need to know about X"
❌ "The definitive guide to X"
❌ "X has changed forever"
❌ "Became" as the main verb of a headline
❌ "We reveal" / "We discover" / "We show"
❌ Headlines starting with "Discover", "Learn", "Meet"
❌ "5 tips for X"
❌ "10 secrets for X"
```

### 7.4 Forbidden closings

```
❌ "Continue to the next slide →"
❌ "Swipe to see more"
❌ "But there's more..."
❌ "It doesn't stop there"
❌ "I hope you liked it!"
❌ "If you have questions, send me a message"
❌ "Thanks for following"
❌ "Don't forget to follow"
❌ "If this content helped you..."
❌ "The question that remains is..."
❌ "In the end..."
❌ "At the end of the day..."
```

CTA is directive, not cordial. No institutional thanks.

### 7.5 Forbidden tics

```
❌ "And this changes everything"
❌ "In the end"
❌ "At the end of the day"
❌ "In an X way" (generic)
❌ "It's clear that X"
❌ "Increasingly"
❌ "Simply"
❌ "Basically"
❌ "In practice" (as opening)
❌ "In summary"
❌ "As we saw"
❌ "The big truth is"
❌ "As X already said"
❌ "It's no wonder that"
```

### 7.6 Data without source (forbidden)

```
❌ "Studies show that..."     → always name the study
❌ "Experts say..."           → always name who
❌ "Many companies..."        → always number or example
❌ "Most people..."           → always percentage
❌ "Recently..."              → always date or period
❌ "In Brazil..."             → always specific data
❌ "Research proves..."       → source + year
```

**Rule:** number + source + year. Without the three, it's not data — it's opinion.

### 7.7 Numeric anglicisms (forbidden)

| ❌ | ✅ |
|---|---|
| "10+ years" | "more than 10 years" |
| "5x bigger" | "five times bigger" |
| "2-3 years" | "two or three years" |
| "40k followers" (in editorial) | "40 thousand followers" |
| "100M users" (in editorial) | "100 million users" |

**Exception:** numeric anglicisms OK in ad/CTA copy (tight space) and in native technical vocabulary ("pricing tier", "100 reqs/sec").

## 8. Forbidden corporate vocabulary (with substitutions)

List of saturated jargon, with preferred colloquial version:

| Forbidden jargon | Replace with |
|---|---|
| Ecosystem | system, market, environment |
| Synergy | integration, collaboration |

## 9. Tonal modulation by context (reference)

Voice is fixed. Tone modulates in **5-7 typical contexts**. The Maestro maps these contexts in DNA.md (section 4.5). Here is the reference typology:

### Tone 1 — Editorial (carousel, long post, blog, deck)

**Definition:** full voice. Journalism applied to the brand's subject. High density, natural connectives, no second person, fact always before opinion.

**Typical opening (structure):**
> "On [specific date], [company/product/phenomenon] [verb of change] [verifiable number/mark]. It wasn't [obvious explanation that will be refuted] — it was [real explanation, underlying mechanism]."

### Tone 2 — Conversational (caption, story, personal email)

**Definition:** voice with 2nd person allowed. More intimate. Shorter sentences. Colloquial connectives ("so", "only that", "then"). One joke per piece is OK.

**Typical opening:**
> "You know that A/B test you run every week and never conclude anything? We used to laugh too. Then we started doing it differently."

### Tone 3 — Commercial (sales page, strong CTA, ad)

**Definition:** voice anchored in benefit. Explicit promise. Embedded social proof. High density — each paragraph carries benefit + proof + action.

**Typical opening (structure):**
> "[Brand] is [concrete positioning + unique differentiator]. In [specific deadline], you [concrete functional result] — without [friction the traditional alternative imposes]."

### Tone 4 — Transactional (confirmation email, receipt, notification)

**Definition:** factual voice. Short. Without flourish. Resolves what the person needs to know, period.

**Typical opening:**
> "Payment confirmed. Access released. Login: [URL]. Next charge: [date]. Anything: [human email]."

### Tone 5 — Crisis (response to controversy, public clarification, retraction)

**Definition:** institutional + assumed voice. Acknowledges, explains, repairs. Without becoming public relations, but with appropriate legal/reputational weight.

**Typical opening:**
> "We were wrong. In the post of [date], we attributed a data point to the wrong source. The correct information comes from [real source]. We've already corrected it in the original post and thank whoever pointed it out."

### Tone 6 — Service (DM, comment, support)

**Definition:** human, patient, resolutive voice. Can have light humor. Without becoming customer success script.

**Typical opening:**
> "Hi. Here's the thing: what happened there is probably [hypothesis]. Try [practical step]. If it doesn't work, send me a print of what shows up and we'll figure it out."

### Tone 7 — Institutional pitch (corporate presentation, B2B deck)

**Definition:** more formal voice. Classic argumentative structure (problem → solution → differentiator → proof → ask). Maintains personality — doesn't become generic corporate.

**Typical opening:**
> "The problem you described appears in 3 out of 4 Brazilian brands with internal creative production. The cause isn't lack of talent — it's lack of system. That's why we built [brand]."

## 10. The 5 final tests before approving copy

Before any text goes out as approved, it passes through 5 tests. Failure in any one = rewrite.

### Test 1 — Editorial anchor test

Does it sound like the brand's reference journalistic outlet (`brand_voice_anchor_editorial`)? Or does it sound like a robotic translation of something American?

### Test 2 — Substitution test

Swap the piece's main subject for another of the same size. If it still "works", the piece is generic — rewrite until it only fits your brand.

### Test 3 — Promise test

Was every claim from the hook fulfilled in the body? If the hook says "three things", are there three things? If it says "what no one comments", is there the revelation?

### Test 4 — Article test

Does every noun have an article? Brazilian Portuguese written without an article sounds truncated, translated, robotic. Verify specifically: "in [noun]" should be "in a [noun]" or "in the [noun]".

### Test 5 — Active binary test

Did you actively search for "not X, but Y", "no X, no Y", "less X, more Y", "in an X way"? If it appears, rewrite in prose with natural connector.
